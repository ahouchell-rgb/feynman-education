# Example Lesson: Rates of Reaction — Effect of Concentration (Year 9, 60 min)

**A fully worked, annotated example lesson built from [the Science Lesson Playbook](./SCIENCE_LESSON_PLAYBOOK.md).**

This is not a template to photocopy — it is a *worked example of lesson design*. Every phase shows exactly what the teacher does and says, followed by a **Design decisions** note explaining *why*, with the research warrant. Section references (§) point to the playbook.

**Lesson context**
- Class: Year 9, mixed attainment, 28 students. Third lesson of the *Rates of Reaction* unit.
- Prior learning: particle model (Y7), acids + carbonates (Y8), collision theory introduced last lesson.
- Core idea today: *increasing concentration increases the frequency of successful collisions, so rate increases — but the total product is fixed by the limiting reactant.*
- Target misconception (from Driver et al., *Making Sense of Secondary Science*): "more concentrated acid → more gas made" (confusing **rate** with **yield**).

---

## 0:00–0:02 — Entry routine

**What happens.** Do Now is already on the board. Teacher stands at the door: *"Good afternoon. Pens out, Do Now, in silence — you have six minutes."* Nothing else is said. Teacher scans, does not begin any conversation.

**Design decisions**
- **Instructions are ≤12 words, delivered once, in formal register.** Lemov's *economy of language* and *Strong Voice* (§3.1): every extra word spends attention (Mccrea, §3.2) and dilutes the signal.
- **Work starts at minute 0, not minute 5.** The lesson's scarcest resource is student thinking time (Willingham: memory is the residue of thought, §2.3). Administration happens *while* students retrieve, not before.

---

## 0:02–0:09 — Retrieval Do Now (6 min write + 3 min check)

**On the board (silent, written, closed-book):**

1. Name the gas produced when an acid reacts with a metal carbonate. *(last week)*
2. State the test for that gas. *(last week)*
3. According to collision theory, what TWO things must happen for particles to react? *(last lesson)*
4. A student says "particles in a solid vibrate but don't move around." True or false — and why? *(last term, particle model)*
5. Write one sentence using the word **concentration** correctly. *(vocabulary, feeds today)*

**Checking (0:08–0:11):** answers via cold call, question-first-name-last, 3-second wait. Q3 goes to mini-whiteboards ("Both things. Show me — 3, 2, 1"). Immediate correction given for every item; teacher notes that four students wrote "particles must touch" for Q3.

**Design decisions**
- **Retrieval, not review-by-re-telling.** Free recall strengthens memory far more than being shown the material again — the testing effect (Roediger & Karpicke 2006; Karpicke & Blunt 2011 showed this holds for science content, §2.6). If the teacher recapped instead, the teacher would be doing the generation (Shimamura's G, §2.5).
- **Question spread is engineered, not random**: last lesson / last week / last term / vocabulary. Spacing effects (Cepeda et al. 2006, 2008, §8.6) and Rosenshine's principles 1 and 10 (daily + weekly/monthly review, §2.1). Q5 deliberately pre-loads today's key term so the explanation doesn't have to teach the word and the concept simultaneously (reduces element interactivity — Sweller, §2.2).
- **Everyone writes; nobody opts out.** Wiliam's no-hands-up rule and all-student response (§5.3, §7.2): 5 hands up = 5 data points; 28 whiteboards = 28.
- **Q4 is a truth-evaluation item, not "define X".** Judging a plausible-but-wrong statement forces deeper processing than reciting, and rehearses the *culture of error* (Lemov, §7.3): wrong statements are objects of analysis, not embarrassment.
- **The teacher notes errors instead of just fixing them.** The four "particles must touch" answers are *evidence* (Black & Wiliam, §7.1) — that phrase gets explicitly addressed at 0:15 ("touching isn't enough — they must collide with enough energy").

---

## 0:09–0:12 — Frame the lesson as a puzzle

**Teacher (slow, quiet, formal register — deliberately dropping pace at the key sentence):**

> "Two identical marble chips. Same size. Same mass. I drop one into acid and it fizzes away in forty seconds. I drop the other into a different acid — same volume — and it takes four *minutes*. **What could possibly be different — and *why* would that change anything?**
> Thirty seconds' silent thinking. Then sixty seconds with your partner. Remember our talk rules: give a reason, challenge politely, agree one answer. I will be cold calling pairs."

Two pairs sampled. Teacher revoices: *"So you're claiming it's about how crowded the acid particles are — have I got that right? Hold that thought. By 3 o'clock you'll be able to prove it."*

**Design decisions**
- **A puzzle, not a lesson objective read aloud.** Willingham (§2.3): curiosity comes from problems of *moderate* difficulty — students can almost answer this from collision theory, which is exactly the sweet spot. The causal question ("why would that change anything?") sets up story structure: conflict → resolution.
- **Think–Pair–Share, tightly timed, with visible ground rules and accountable sharing.** Lyman (1981) for the structure; Mercer (§4.4) for the finding that group talk only works when exploratory-talk ground rules are taught and enforced; cold-calling the share makes rehearsal accountable (Lemov's ratio, §4.6).
- **Revoicing** ("so you're claiming… have I got that right?") is Michaels & O'Connor's talk move (§4.5): it dignifies a half-formed idea, models precise language, and keeps ownership with the student.
- **Voice drops rather than rises at the key question.** Lemov's *quiet power* (§3.1) and the prosodic signalling point (§3.2): the register shift itself marks "this is the important bit."

## 0:12–0:15 — Predict (P of POE), before anything is mixed

**Under the visualiser:** two beakers labelled **0.5 M acid** and **2.0 M acid**, identical marble chips, two gas syringes. Nothing is mixed yet.

**Teacher:** "Before I touch anything. On whiteboards, two things: **(1)** which syringe fills *faster*, and **(2)** which reaction makes *more gas in total* — with one reason. You have 90 seconds. No conferring — I want *your* prediction."

Boards up on signal. Teacher scans: nearly all say 2.0 M is faster; **about half predict 2.0 M also makes more gas in total.** Teacher says only: *"Interesting — the room disagrees on question 2. Let's find out."* (Does **not** reveal the answer.)

**Design decisions**
- **Prediction is written, individual, and committed *before* observation.** White & Gunstone's Predict–Observe–Explain (§8.3): the written commitment is what makes a discrepant result produce cognitive conflict (Posner et al.'s conditions for conceptual change, §8.1). Crouch, Fagen, Callan & Mazur (2004) showed experimentally that students who predict before a demo learn significantly more than students who just watch — and passive watching was barely better than no demo at all.
- **The prediction question is split into rate vs. total gas on purpose.** That is precisely the misconception boundary (rate ≠ yield). The demo is *designed as an argument against a specific documented misconception* (Driver, §8.1) — not as a generic "engaging starter."
- **The teacher withholds the answer and names the disagreement.** Disagreement is the hook (Willingham's conflict; Mazur's peer-instruction logic, §8.3). Resolving it now would waste the conflict the demo is about to settle.
- **A demo rather than a class practical — deliberately.** Abrahams & Millar (§8.2): practical work is only effective in the "domain of ideas" when the thinking is engineered around it. Today the *idea* is the goal; a whole-class practical here would spend 20 minutes on apparatus ("hands-on") and bury the reasoning ("minds-on"). The class practical comes next lesson, *after* the idea is secure, when students test surface area — with a purpose they can now articulate.

## 0:15–0:22 — Observe, then explain in small steps (live drawing under the visualiser)

**What happens.** Both reactions started simultaneously; syringes visible under the camera. While gas collects, the teacher draws — **live, one element at a time** — two particle boxes on paper under the visualiser:

1. Draws the dilute box: a few acid particles among water particles. **Stops.** "Sana — what do I add to make this the 2.0 M box?" *(wait 3s)*
2. Draws the concentrated box. "Whiteboards: same volume — which box has more acid particles per cm³? 3-2-1 show."
3. Adds the marble chip surface to both. "Jayden — a collision happens when…? *(wait)* …and last lesson we said touching isn't enough because…?"
4. Annotates arrows for collisions. "In which box are collisions with the surface *more frequent*? Why — Amara? *(wait)* Say it again with the word **frequency** in it."
5. Only now writes the causal chain, one line, as students say it: *more particles per cm³ → more frequent collisions → more successful collisions per second → faster rate.*

By now the syringes show it: **2.0 M filled fast — but both syringes stop at the same volume.** Teacher points at both, silent, three full seconds. Then:

> "Half of you predicted the strong acid would make more gas. That is a completely reasonable prediction — here is why it's tempting: more acid particles, so surely more gas. But look at the syringes. What actually ran out first in both beakers? *(wait — cold call)* The **marble** was the limiting reactant. Concentration changed how *fast* we got the gas. It did not change how *much*. Rate and amount are different things — that is today's whole lesson in one sentence."

**Design decisions**
- **Drawn live, not shown finished on a slide.** A completed diagram arrives all at once — high element interactivity with no guidance about where to look (Sweller, §2.2). Live drawing segments the content (Mayer's segmenting principle), directs attention to exactly one element at a time (signalling), and creates natural question points at every stage (Boxer's visualiser argument, §6.1). Speech accompanies the drawing simultaneously — temporal contiguity (Mayer, §6.3) — and the diagram persists, countering the transient-information effect of spoken-only explanation.
- **A checking question roughly every 90 seconds of explanation.** Rosenshine's small steps + principle 3 (§2.1, §5.1): the most effective teachers in the process–product studies asked ~24+ questions per lesson vs ~9 for the least effective (Evertson et al. 1980). Questions are cold-called, question-first-name-last, with 3-second wait time (Rowe, §4.2) so all 28 students must generate an answer before anyone is named.
- **"Say it again with *frequency* in it"** — Sherrington's "say it again, better" (§5.3) plus Quigley's vocabulary point (§8.5): first answers are drafts; the redraft rehearses the technical register. Note the concept came *before* the word was demanded (Engelmann: concept first, label second).
- **The misconception is stated, dignified, and refuted explicitly** — "here is why it's tempting" — rather than silently corrected. Refutation-style explanation outperforms simply stating the correct idea (Tippett 2010 review, §8.1), because unaddressed frameworks survive underneath new teaching (Nuthall, §7.1; Chi's point that this is a category error: treating rate as if it were amount, §8.1).
- **Three seconds of silent pointing at the syringes** before speaking: Wait Time as a tool the *teacher* uses on the class (Rowe's Wait Time 2 logic, §4.2) — the discrepant data does the persuading; narrating over it would steal the moment.

## 0:22–0:25 — Hinge question (the lesson's decision point)

**On the board; students answer A/B/C/D on whiteboards, 30 seconds, simultaneous reveal:**

> Marble chips react with **excess** dilute acid. If I double the concentration of the acid, what happens?
> **A.** Double the total gas is produced
> **B.** The gas is produced faster, but the total is the same
> **C.** The gas is produced faster AND there is more of it
> **D.** No change until the temperature also increases

Each distractor is a diagnosis: **A/C** = rate–yield confusion persisting (C is the "hedge" version); **D** = "only heat changes rate" (a documented framework).

**Decision rules, planned in advance:**
- **>80% B** → move to modelling (0:25).
- **30–70% B** → 90-second Mazur peer-instruction round: *"Find someone who answered differently. Convince them. Revote."* Then one cold-called justification per answer, then proceed or re-teach.
- **<30% B** → re-teach with a new representation (bead-and-cup limiting-reactant model), skip one independent-practice question to buy the time.

**Today:** 21/28 choose B → brief cold call on *why A is wrong* ("what ran out?"), then proceed. The 7 students who chose A or C are noted by name for targeted circulation at 0:35.

**Design decisions**
- **This is a hinge, not a quiz**: it exists to make a *teaching decision* in under a minute (Wiliam, §5.2: all answer <1 min, teacher interprets in seconds, and wrong answers must be *interpretable* — hence misconception-mapped distractors from the Driver literature).
- **Distractors are designed so students can't be right for the wrong reason.** Option C exists precisely to catch students who learned "faster" but kept "more" — a plain A/B item would hide them.
- **The contingency plans are written before the lesson.** A check for understanding you can't act on is ritual, not formative assessment (Wiliam, §7.4). The 30–70% band triggers peer instruction because that's the range where discussion between differing answers is most productive (Crouch & Mazur 2001, §8.3).
- **Simultaneous reveal on signal** (Lemov, *standardise the format*, §7.2) so answers are independent — a staggered reveal lets students copy rather than commit.

## 0:25–0:35 — Modelling: rate graphs (I do → we do → faded)

**Under the visualiser:**

1. **I do (4 min).** Teacher plots gas volume vs time for the 2.0 M run from the demo's readings, narrating decisions *and self-checks* aloud: *"Before I read anything off a rate graph, I find where the curve goes flat — that's the moment a reactant ran out. I always check that first, because every question about 'total gas' is answered there, and every question about 'fastest rate' is answered at the start, where it's steepest."* Sketches the 0.5 M curve on the same axes: shallower, **same plateau height**.
2. **We do (3 min).** Second pair of curves (different reaction, same structure) completed via cold-called steps: "What do I check first — Marcus? … Where's the fastest rate — how do you *know*?"
3. **Faded (3 min).** Third graph on the handout with the final two steps missing (plateau reading + comparing initial gradients). Students complete on whiteboards; boards up; two errors surfaced and fixed publicly.

**Design decisions**
- **Worked example before problem solving.** For novices, studying a worked example outperforms solving the equivalent problem (Sweller & Cooper 1985, §2.2/§6.2) — problem-solving search overloads working memory and leaves nothing for learning the structure.
- **The narration is metacognitive, not just procedural.** "I always check the plateau first, *because*…" makes expert decision-making visible — cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown & Newman, §6.1) and Rosenshine's principle 4 (think-alouds as models). The self-explanation prompts in the We-do phase ("how do you *know*?") work because learning from examples depends on the quality of self-explanation (Chi et al. 1989; Renkl 1997, §6.2).
- **Fading, not a cliff.** Backward-faded examples (complete → last steps missing → full problem) outperform an abrupt jump from example to problem (Renkl & Atkinson, §6.2), and set up the expertise-reversal transition (§2.2): scaffolds are removed as competence grows *within the same 10 minutes*.
- **Both curves on one pair of axes, plateau explicitly equal.** The graph *is* the refutation of the misconception in a second representation — dual coding the causal chain from 0:15 (Paivio/Caviglioli, §6.3). Same idea, third encounter, different form (Nuthall's 3-encounter finding, §7.1).

## 0:35–0:50 — Independent practice (interleaved, ramped)

**Handout, silent start, 8 questions:**
- Q1–3: routine graph readings (which is faster, when did it finish, how much gas).
- Q4–5: explain-why items requiring the causal chain in full sentences, word bank given (*frequency, successful, limiting*).
- Q6: "A student says doubling concentration doubles the gas produced. Explain what is right and what is wrong about this claim." *(direct misconception item)*
- Q7 (interleaved): conservation-of-mass calculation in a rates context (from two topics ago).
- Q8 (transfer): sketch the curve for the *same* mass of marble as **powder** — and justify.

**Teacher circulates with one pre-decided target:** the error of reading "fastest rate" anywhere but the initial gradient. Light annotations only ("check your first move"). The 7 students flagged at the hinge get visited first. At 0:45, **show-call**: one strong Q4 answer and one improvable one projected side by side (with permission, names optional): *"Both of these know the science. One of them would get the mark. Thirty seconds — what's the difference?"* → class identifies the missing causal link → **everyone gets 2 minutes to upgrade their own Q4/Q6 using that check.*

**Design decisions**
- **Success rate is engineered at roughly 80%.** Q1–3 nearly everyone gets (fluency, Rosenshine's principle 7, §2.1); Q6–8 stretch. All-easy teaches nothing; all-hard practises errors.
- **Interleaving is planned, not decorative.** Q7 forces method *selection*, not just execution — interleaved practice loses on today's performance but wins on delayed tests (Rohrer & Taylor 2007, §8.6). One item is enough; this early in the unit, most practice is still blocked.
- **Q6 makes the misconception itself the object of analysis.** Evaluating a wrong claim is a refutation task the *student* performs (Tippett, §8.1) — fourth encounter with the core idea.
- **Circulation hunts one error** (Lemov, *tracking not watching*, §7.2): deciding in advance what you're looking for turns wandering into data collection; targeting the hinge-flagged seven is the responsive-teaching payoff of 0:22.
- **Show-call with an improvable (not "bad") answer** transmits the standard better than rubric language can (Sadler 1989; Christodoulou, §6.4) — students must hold a concept of quality to self-assess — and the mandatory 2-minute upgrade makes the feedback *more work for the recipient than the donor* (Wiliam's rule, §7.5). It only works atop the culture of error built since minute 2 (Lemov, §7.3).
- **Q8 seeds next lesson's practical.** The prediction will be tested with real apparatus next time — which is what gives that practical an ideas-purpose (Abrahams & Millar's level-2 effectiveness, §8.2).

## 0:50–0:55 — Exit ticket (collected, read tonight, drives tomorrow)

**Three questions, individual, silent, on slips:**

1. Doubling the concentration of the acid makes the reaction ______ but does not change ______. *(core)*
2. **Explain why** in terms of particles. Use *frequency* in your answer. *(causal why — the residue we want)*
3. Two identical antacid tablets: one whole, one crushed, same water. **Predict** which fizzes away first — one sentence of reasoning. *(transfer + hook for next lesson; not marked right/wrong yet)*

**After school (10 min, part of the lesson system):** slips sorted into three piles — *secure / rate-yield confusion persists / mechanism vague*. The piles write tomorrow's Do Now Q1–2 and decide whether the surface-area lesson opens with a 3-minute re-teach. The core causal chain is already scheduled to reappear in the Week 4 spaced quiz and the surface-area explanation — encounters four and five.

**Design decisions**
- **The exit ticket's value is the *reading*, not the ritual** (Wiliam, §7.4): if the piles never changed the next lesson, this would be theatre. The sort is deliberately three-way because the two failure modes (misconception vs. vague mechanism) need *different* fixes.
- **Q2 targets the causal chain, not a definition**, because that's what students should have been thinking about all lesson — and memory is the residue of thought (Willingham, §2.3).
- **Q3 ends the lesson on an open prediction** — Willingham's moderate-difficulty curiosity again, and the P of next lesson's POE already banked.
- **Recurrences are planned now.** Nuthall (§7.1): durable learning needs ~3+ complete, spaced encounters, and different students are missing different pieces — one great lesson is structurally insufficient, so the unit map, not the lesson, is the real unit of design.

---

## 0:55–0:60 — Student-generated close

**What happens.** "Books closed. Sixty seconds: write today's whole argument from memory — beaker to graph. Go." Then pairs check against the visualiser diagram (put back under the camera); teacher cold calls one full run-through, previews tomorrow's crushed-tablet question, and runs the exit routine. Ends on time.

**Design decisions**
- **Students generate the summary; the teacher does not recap.** A teacher recap is the teacher retrieving. The brain dump is free recall — the single highest-utility technique in Dunlosky et al.'s (2013) review, in its cheapest form (Agarwal & Bain's "brain dumps", §2.6; Shimamura's Generate, §2.5).
- **Checking against the persistent diagram** closes the loop on the live drawing (§6.1): the artefact built at 0:15 becomes the answer key at 0:56.
- **Ending on time, with a routine, in formal register** is a behaviour *and* attention decision (Lemov; Mccrea): the last minute sets the entry cost of the next lesson.

## The lesson's question budget (as delivered)

| Phase | Written Qs (all students) | Oral Qs (cold call/bounce) | Type balance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Do Now + check | 5 | ~6 | recall + one evaluate |
| Puzzle frame | — | ~3 | open causal |
| Predict | 2 | — | prediction w/ reason |
| Explanation | 2 (whiteboard) | ~7 | mix: recall + why chasers |
| Hinge | 1 (MCQ) | ~3 | diagnostic |
| Modelling | ~4 (faded steps) | ~8 | process ("how do you know?") |
| Independent | 8 | ~6 (circulating) | ramped, incl. refutation + transfer |
| Exit + close | 3 + brain dump | ~2 | core, why, predict |
| **Total** | **~25 written** | **~35 oral** | |

~35 oral questions in 60 minutes sits at the "most effective teacher" end of Evertson et al.'s (1980) distribution (§5.1); ~25 written all-student responses is what makes the checking claim honest (Nuthall, §7.1) — the oral questions sample depth, the written ones sample *everyone*.

## What was deliberately left out (negative design decisions)

- **No class practical today** — the idea had to come first; hands-on without a question to answer is Abrahams & Millar's level-1 trap (§8.2). The practical is next lesson, now that students own a prediction worth testing.
- **No lesson-objective copying, no "any questions?", no hands up for answers, no text-heavy slides read aloud, no grades on the exit ticket** — each is a named research pitfall (§10): self-report isn't evidence (Rosenshine; Lemov), hands-up samples the confident (Wiliam), read-aloud slides violate Mayer's redundancy principle, and Butler (1988) showed grades wipe out the effect of comments.
- **No video clip or anecdote about explosions** — seductive details measurably reduce learning of the target content (Garner et al.; Rey, §3.2). The demo *is* the spectacle, and the spectacle is the content.
