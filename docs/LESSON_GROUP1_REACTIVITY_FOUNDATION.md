# Lesson Playbook: Why Group 1 Reactivity Increases Down the Group

**Year 10/11 · AQA Combined Science Trilogy (Foundation) · very weak class · 60 minutes**

Built from [the Science Lesson Playbook](./SCIENCE_LESSON_PLAYBOOK.md) (§ references) and formatted like the [annotated example lesson](./EXAMPLE_SCIENCE_LESSON.md). Every phase gives what to do and say, then **Design decisions** explaining why — including how each choice is adapted for a very weak Foundation class.

---

## Before you read the plan: what changes for a very weak Foundation class

The research base doesn't change for weaker students — it applies *harder*:

- **Explicit, small-step instruction benefits the weakest students most.** Rosenshine (§2.1) built his principles largely from studies of what worked for struggling learners; Black & Wiliam (§7.1) found formative assessment helps low attainers *disproportionately*, narrowing the gap.
- **Assume less working-memory headroom, not less capability.** Every step below is smaller, every check more frequent, and success rate is engineered *above* Rosenshine's ~80% — nearer 90% during guided practice — because for anxious, low-confidence students, early failure shuts down participation (Wiliam; Lemov's culture of error, §7.3).
- **Vocabulary is taught, not assumed** (Quigley, §8.5): five words carry this whole lesson — *nucleus, outer electron, attraction, reactive, group* — and they are retrieved, chorally decoded, and demanded in answers.
- **Nothing is copied, nothing is read off dense slides, and the teacher never talks for more than ~3 minutes without an all-class response** (Mayer redundancy, §6.3; Rosenshine principle 3).

**The AQA target (Trilogy 5.1.2.5, Foundation):** describe the reactions of Li/Na/K with water; state that reactivity increases down Group 1; explain the trend in terms of the outer electron being further from the nucleus, so less strongly attracted, so lost more easily. (Shielding is Chemistry-only — do **not** teach it; it adds load with no marks.)

**Target misconceptions** (Driver tradition, §8.1 — plus anthropomorphism per Taber's work on chemistry misconceptions):
1. "Potassium is more reactive because it's heavier / hits the water harder."
2. "It's more reactive because it has *more electrons* (more to lose)."
3. "Atoms *want* a full outer shell" — the desire framing that blocks the actual mechanism (attraction/distance).
4. "The metal dissolves" (change of state) rather than *reacts* (new substances made).

**Core causal chain (the whole lesson in one line):**
*bigger atom → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker attraction → electron lost more easily → more reactive.*

## 0:00–0:02 — Entry

Do Now on desks as they enter. At the door, formal register, twelve words: *"Good morning. Sit down, pens out, Do Now, in silence. Six minutes."*

**Design decisions.** Economy of language and routine (Lemov, §3.1); thinking starts at minute 0 (Willingham, §2.3). With a weak class the *predictability* of the routine matters even more — novelty is load (Sweller, §2.2).

---

## 0:02–0:10 — Retrieval Do Now (printed slip, big font, 5 items)

1. Label the diagram: **nucleus** / **electron** / **shell**. *(labels provided in a word bank — matching, not recalling from nothing)*
2. Sodium's electronic structure is 2,8,1. Circle the **outer** electron on the diagram.
3. The nucleus has a **positive / negative** charge. *(circle one)*
4. Metals react by **losing / gaining** electrons. *(circle one)*
5. Sodium is in Group **1 / 2 / 7**. How can you tell from 2,8,1?

**Check (0:08–0:10):** answers cold-called, question first, 3-second wait; Q3 and Q4 also chorally repeated by the whole class ("Metals react by… everyone: *losing electrons*"). Any wrong answer gets a warm correction and a return: *"Hold on — I'll come back to you to say the full answer."* And you do.

**Design decisions**
- **Every item is a prerequisite for today, not general revision.** Today's explanation is *built from* nucleus-charge + outer-electron + losing-electrons. If those aren't retrievable, the explanation has nowhere to attach (Shimamura's Relate, §2.5). This is retrieval practice (Roediger & Karpicke, §2.6) doing double duty as load-reduction.
- **Recognition formats (circle / match / label) instead of free recall.** For a very weak class the Do Now must run at ~90% success (Rosenshine principle 7, §2.1) or it teaches "I can't do science" in the first five minutes. Difficulty is raised *within* the lesson, not at the door.
- **Choral response for the two facts the lesson leans on hardest** (nucleus positive; metals lose electrons): Engelmann's high-frequency automaticity tool (§7.2) — response-per-minute far above hand-raising, zero social risk.
- **"I'll come back to you" — and returning — is Lemov's *No Opt Out* (§5.3):** the sequence ends with the weakest student succeeding out loud.

## 0:10–0:13 — Frame as a puzzle (not an objective)

Hold up (or show under the visualiser) three sealed sample tubes / photos: lithium, sodium, potassium.

> "Three metals from the same family — Group 1, the far-left column. Same family, same reactions… but NOT the same *violence*. One of these fizzes politely. One whizzes around and bursts into flame. **By the end of today you'll be able to say which — and, more importantly, WHY.** And the why is worth 4 marks on your exam, and I promise you can all get them."

**Design decisions**
- **Puzzle framing** creates the moderate-difficulty curiosity gap (Willingham, §2.3): they can see the pattern is coming but can't yet explain it.
- **The explicit "you can all get these 4 marks" line is a motivation move for a low-confidence class** (Mccrea, §3.2): the goal is framed as attainable and near-term. Weak Foundation classes have usually *practised failure* on "explain" questions; naming the prize reframes it.
- **No lesson-objective copying** — five minutes of transcription buys zero thinking (§10).

---

## 0:13–0:17 — Predict (P of POE), before any water is touched

Under the visualiser: trough of water with universal indicator, safety screen up. Teacher shows the periodic table, points down Group 1: Li → Na → K.

**Whiteboards, 60 seconds, no conferring:** *"I will put lithium in first, then sodium. Prediction: when we get to POTASSIUM — third one down — will it be MORE violent or LESS violent than sodium? Write MORE or LESS and one word why."*

Boards up on "3-2-1 show me." Scan. Expect most to write MORE (fine); the *reasons* are the data — you'll see "heavier," "stronger," "more electrons." Say only: *"Nearly everyone says more. Your reasons disagree with each other. Watch closely — then we'll find the real why."*

**Design decisions**
- **Predict-before-observe** is the single most evidenced way to make a demo teach (White & Gunstone POE, §8.3; Crouch et al. 2004: predicting before a demo produced significant learning, passive watching almost none).
- **Binary prediction (MORE/LESS) plus one word** keeps the writing demand near zero — the *commitment* is what matters, not prose (§8.3). For weak writers, any prediction task that needs sentences becomes a writing task, and non-writers opt out.
- **Harvesting wrong reasons without correcting them yet**: those one-word reasons are misconceptions 1–3 arriving on schedule (Driver, §8.1). They become the hinge distractors at 0:33 — and naming the disagreement, not the answer, preserves the conflict the demo will resolve (Posner et al., §8.1).

## 0:17–0:25 — Observe: the demo, run as a pattern-builder

Behind the screen, with running commentary in formal register, deliberately slow:

1. **Lithium** first. Narrate ONLY observations, and make students supply them: *"What can you see — Chloe? (fizzing) What's the gas, if metals push hydrogen out of water — everyone, choral: HY-DRO-GEN."* Note indicator turning purple/blue: *"The water is becoming an ALKALI. That's why this family is called the ALKALI METALS."*
2. **Sodium** second: *"Same tests. Whiteboards: is this MORE or LESS violent than lithium? Show me."* (melts into a ball, whizzes, faster fizz).
3. **Pause before potassium.** *"Two data points. Li: fizz. Na: whizz. Potassium is next down. Final prediction, boards up: what will we SEE?"* Then potassium (or the CLEAPSS-compliant video if your school doesn't demo K): lilac flame, immediate ignition.
4. Build the results table **live under the visualiser** as you go — three rows, two columns (metal / what we saw), students' own observation words.

**Design decisions**
- **A teacher demo, not a class practical — non-negotiable here for safety, but also pedagogically right:** the idea (a *trend*) only exists across all three metals seen in controlled sequence; Abrahams & Millar (§8.2) — the thinking around the practical, not the touching, is what teaches. (Video for potassium is fine: Crouch et al. found the learning lives in the *prediction and discussion*, not physical presence.)
- **The demo is sequenced as an inductive pattern machine**: predict → observe → update → predict again. The second prediction (before K) is made *with better evidence*, and nearly everyone now gets it right — an engineered success moment for a low-confidence class (Rosenshine principle 7).
- **Observation words are the students', recorded live** (Boxer, visualiser as shared thinking surface, §6.1) — the table persists, beating the transient-information effect (§2.2), and it becomes the left-hand column of the explanation at 0:25.
- **Choral decode of the two polysyllables students will need to say and write** — *hy-dro-gen*, later *po-tas-si-um, at-trac-tion* (Quigley/Raichura routine, §8.5): weak readers avoid words they can't pronounce; 5 seconds of choral work removes that barrier.
- **"Dissolves" is banned live**: the first time a student says the sodium "dissolved," respond: *"It's GONE, yes — but it didn't hide in the water like sugar does. It REACTED — it turned into new substances: an alkali, and the hydrogen we burned. That's misconception number one — write it on my wall of traps."* (Refutation move, §8.1.)

## 0:25–0:33 — Explain: the mechanism, drawn live, one element at a time

The heart of the lesson. Under the visualiser, plain paper, thick pen. **Slow down; drop volume; this is the 4-mark bit** (register shift — Lemov §3.1).

1. Draw **lithium**: small circle nucleus marked **+**, two small shells, ONE outer electron marked **−**. *"Whiteboards: what charge is the nucleus? Show me. What charge is the electron? Show me."*
2. Draw an arrow between them: *"Positive and negative… everyone: ATTRACT. The nucleus is HOLDING ONTO that outer electron. Like this —"* **magnet and paperclip, live under the camera**: paperclip close to the magnet — *"try to pull it away — hard."*
3. Draw **potassium** next to it: same + nucleus, but FOUR shells — outer electron visibly far away. *"Same one electron in the outer shell — that's why they're in the same family. But look how FAR it is now."* Magnet again: paperclip held far away — barely held, falls.
4. Cold call the chain, one link per student, 3-second waits: *"Bigger atom, so the outer electron is… (further from the nucleus). So the attraction is… (weaker). So the electron is lost… (more easily). And a metal that loses its electron easily is MORE… (reactive)."*
5. Write the chain as **five boxes with arrows** under the drawings — students copy ONLY this chain (one minute, it's five short phrases):
   **bigger atom → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker attraction → electron lost more easily → MORE reactive**
6. Kill the "wants" framing explicitly: *"Notice what I did NOT say. I didn't say potassium WANTS to lose its electron. Atoms don't want anything — they're not alive. It's just a weaker grip. Weaker grip, electron lost, job done. If you write 'wants a full shell' in the exam, you get zero for that line."*

**Design decisions**
- **Live drawing, not a finished slide diagram**: segmenting, signalling, contiguity (Mayer §6.3); one element at a time is the only pace a weak class's working memory can follow (Sweller §2.2); the drawing persists as the reference for everything after (Boxer §6.1). Two atoms side by side is a minimally-different **example pair** — the Engelmann move (§2.4): the ONLY visible difference is distance, so distance is the only available explanation.
- **The magnet-paperclip analogy is concrete, physical, and shown — not described.** Dual coding (Paivio/Caviglioli §6.3) plus a physical referent for the abstract word *attraction*. State its limit in one line ("electrons aren't paperclips — but the grip idea is right") — analogies unpoliced become next year's misconceptions (Driver §8.1).
- **The causal chain is the only thing copied all lesson**, and it's copied *after* being built orally — generation before transcription (Shimamura §2.5). Five boxes ≈ the exam mark scheme's four creditable steps: the note-form IS the answer plan.
- **Anthropomorphism is refuted by name** ("atoms don't want anything") — the refutation approach (Tippett §8.1); Taber's chemistry-misconception research shows the full-shell "desire" framework actively blocks the attraction mechanism you're teaching, so leaving it standing costs you the 4 marks.
- **Question every ~90 seconds, all cold call + choral, ~90% success** — Rosenshine 3, 6, 7 tuned for this class (§5.1, and the Foundation preamble above).

## 0:33–0:36 — Hinge question (decision point)

Boards, A/B/C/D, 30 seconds, simultaneous reveal:

> **Why is potassium more reactive than lithium?**
> **A.** Potassium atoms are heavier, so they hit the water harder
> **B.** Potassium has more electrons, so it has more to lose
> **C.** Potassium's outer electron is further from the nucleus, so it is lost more easily
> **D.** Potassium wants a full outer shell more than lithium does

Every distractor is one of the harvested misconceptions from 0:13 (A = heavier/force; B = more electrons; D = wants). **Decision rules:** ≥80% C → proceed. 30–70% → 90-second convince-your-neighbour, revote (Mazur §8.3). <30% → redraw the two atoms with ONLY the distance difference highlighted, re-chain chorally, re-ask.

**Design decisions.** Wiliam's hinge design (§5.2): all answer in under a minute, wrong answers are *diagnoses* not noise, and the responsive move is planned before the lesson, not improvised (§7.4). For this class the distractors deliberately use the students' own words from the prediction round — they are voting on their *own* earlier theories, which is the conceptual-change moment (Posner et al., §8.1) made visible.

---

## 0:36–0:48 — Guided → faded practice: writing the 4-marker

1. **I do (3 min), visualiser:** the actual AQA-style question — *"Explain why potassium is more reactive than lithium. (4)"* Teacher writes a full answer live, narrating: *"I don't start writing. I start by pointing at my chain. Every box = a sentence. Watch: box one…"* Four short sentences appear, each tied to a box.
2. **We do (3 min):** same question, new pair — *sodium vs lithium* — answer built from cold-called sentences, teacher scribing.
3. **Faded (6 min):** students write their own for *"Explain why sodium is MORE reactive than lithium"* with the **sentence frame** on the board:
   - *As you go down Group 1, the atoms get ________.*
   - *The outer electron is ________ from the nucleus.*
   - *So the attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron is ________.*
   - *So the outer electron is lost more ________, which makes the metal ________ reactive.*
   Word bank: *bigger · further · weaker · easily · more.* **Stretch (do NOT gate on it):** rewrite without the frame; and a word equation with scaffolding — *sodium + water → sodium ________ + hydrogen.*
4. Circulate hunting ONE pre-decided error: answers that describe ("it fizzes more") instead of explain (no *attraction* link). At 0:45, **show-call** one strong answer: *"Count the boxes in Jordan's answer — all four. That's the 4 marks."* Two minutes: everyone checks their own answer against the chain, adds any missing box.

**Design decisions**
- **Worked example → completion → frame is the fading ladder** (Sweller & Cooper; Renkl & Atkinson, §6.2), and for a very weak class the frame is the difference between practising the science and staring at a blank line. The frame is *removed* for the stretch, never made permanent — expertise reversal (§2.2) says scaffolds must fade or they cap growth.
- **The chain-to-sentences routine is metacognitive modelling** (Collins et al., §6.1): what you're actually teaching in step 1 is *how an expert converts a diagram into exam prose* — the transferable skill this class lacks most.
- **Word equations are stretch, not core — a deliberate curriculum triage.** Engelmann's mastery-before-movement (§2.4): one secure causal chain beats two shaky objectives. The reactions' products get their own consolidation next lesson; today everything serves the trend explanation.
- **Show-call counts boxes, publicly and warmly** (Lemov §6.4; Sadler/Christodoulou §6.4): quality is *shown* as a checkable structure, not described in rubric-speak — and self-checking against the chain makes the feedback more work for the recipient than the donor (Wiliam §7.5). No grades on any of it (Butler 1988, §7.5).

---

## 0:48–0:55 — Exit ticket (slips, silent, collected)

1. Circle: as you go DOWN Group 1, reactivity gets **higher / lower**.
2. **Explain why** in one or two sentences. You must use the words **outer electron**, **nucleus**, **attraction**. *(frame available on request — note who asks)*
3. **Predict:** Rubidium is BELOW potassium in Group 1. What would it do in water? One sentence.

**Tonight (10 min):** sort into three piles — *secure / describes-but-no-mechanism / "wants-a-full-shell" or heavier-misconception intact*. Piles write tomorrow's Do Now and decide whether you re-teach the chain for 3 minutes. The chain is scheduled to reappear in next week's spaced starter and again when Group 7 trends are taught (where the logic runs in reverse — gaining electrons — so today's chain is the anchor).

**Design decisions.** Q3 is the actual AQA Foundation skill (*predict from a given trend*) and tells you whether the pattern generalised beyond the three metals seen. The three-pile sort is Wiliam's read-then-act rule (§7.4); the planned reappearances are Nuthall's three-encounters finding (§7.1) — and flagging who still needs the frame is your differentiation data, gathered without a single worksheet variant.

---

## 0:55–0:60 — Student-generated close

*"Books closed. Whiteboards. Draw my two atoms — small one, big one — and the five boxes. From memory. Two minutes. Go."* Boards up; compare against the visualiser drawing (put it back under the camera); choral run-through of the chain one final time; preview: *"Tomorrow — the OPPOSITE family. Group 7. Where the trend flips upside-down, and you already own the reason."*

**Design decisions.** Free-recall brain dump of the *diagram + chain* is generation in both codes — verbal and visual (Dunlosky's practice testing, §8.6; Paivio §6.3; Shimamura's Generate/Evaluate §2.5). The teacher recapping instead would be the teacher doing the remembering (§2.6). Ending by previewing Group 7 as "the same reason, flipped" plants the relational link that turns two topics into one schema (Rosenshine principle 10, §2.1).

## Question budget (as planned)

| Phase | Written/all-student | Oral (cold call/choral) |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now + check | 5 | ~7 (incl. 2 choral) |
| Puzzle + predict | 2 (whiteboard) | ~3 |
| Demo | 3 (whiteboard) | ~8 (incl. chorals) |
| Explain (live drawing) | 3 (whiteboard) | ~9 |
| Hinge | 1 (MCQ) | ~3 |
| Guided/faded writing | 1 extended + frame | ~8 |
| Exit + close | 3 + brain dump | ~4 |
| **Total** | **~18 all-student responses** | **~40+ oral** |

That oral count sits at the effective-teacher end of Evertson et al.'s distribution (§5.1); for a weak class the balance deliberately shifts toward **whiteboard + choral** (zero-writing-cost, zero-social-risk responses) and away from extended writing — except the one 4-marker, which gets twelve fully scaffolded minutes.

## Deliberately left out

- **Shielding** — Chemistry-only; adds load, earns no Trilogy marks (curriculum triage, Engelmann §2.4).
- **Class practical** — safety, and the trend only exists across a controlled sequence (Abrahams & Millar §8.2).
- **Balanced symbol equations** — next lesson, after the trend is secure (mastery before movement).
- **A reactivity video montage of caesium explosions** as an opener — pure seductive detail (Garner/Rey §3.2): they'd remember the explosion and not the electron. The prediction-loaded demo IS the spectacle, and here the spectacle *is* the content.
- **Copying objectives, "any questions?", hands-up, grades** — the standing pitfalls (§10).

**Safety note:** Li/Na demo behind a safety screen with eye protection per CLEAPSS guidance; potassium only if your school's risk assessment allows — otherwise use video at step 3 of the demo, which the prediction structure fully survives.
