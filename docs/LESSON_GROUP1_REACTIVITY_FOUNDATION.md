# Lesson Playbook: Why Group 1 Reactivity Increases Down the Group

**Year 10/11 · AQA Combined Science Trilogy (Foundation) · very weak class · 60 minutes**

Built from [the Science Lesson Playbook](./SCIENCE_LESSON_PLAYBOOK.md) (§ references) and formatted like the [annotated example lesson](./EXAMPLE_SCIENCE_LESSON.md). Every phase gives what to do and say, then **Design decisions** explaining why — including how each choice is adapted for a very weak Foundation class.

**Lesson context.** Last lesson the class **saw the reactions**: Li/Na/K in water, the fizzing → whizzing → flame pattern, hydrogen produced, alkali formed. Today is a pure **explanation lesson**: they know *what* happens; today is *why*. That prior lesson is an asset, not lost time — today is the **second of Nuthall's three-plus encounters** with the trend (§7.1), and the freed demo time is reinvested where a weak Foundation class needs it most: guided practice on the 4-mark explanation.

---

## Before you read the plan: what changes for a very weak Foundation class

The research base doesn't change for weaker students — it applies *harder*:

- **Explicit, small-step instruction benefits the weakest students most.** Rosenshine (§2.1) built his principles largely from studies of what worked for struggling learners; Black & Wiliam (§7.1) found formative assessment helps low attainers *disproportionately*, narrowing the gap.
- **Assume less working-memory headroom, not less capability.** Every step below is smaller, every check more frequent, and success rate is engineered *above* Rosenshine's ~80% — nearer 90% during guided practice — because for anxious, low-confidence students, early failure shuts down participation (Wiliam; Lemov's culture of error, §7.3).
- **Vocabulary is taught, not assumed** (Quigley, §8.5): five words carry this whole lesson — *nucleus, outer electron, attraction, reactive, group* — and they are retrieved, chorally decoded, and demanded in answers.
- **Nothing is copied, nothing is read off dense slides, and the teacher never talks for more than ~3 minutes without an all-class response** (Mayer redundancy, §6.3; Rosenshine principle 3).

**The AQA target (Trilogy 5.1.2.5, Foundation):** describe the reactions of Li/Na/K with water (done last lesson — retrieved today); state that reactivity increases down Group 1; explain the trend in terms of the outer electron being further from the nucleus, so less strongly attracted, so lost more easily. (Shielding is Chemistry-only — do **not** teach it; it adds load with no marks.)

**Target misconceptions** (Driver tradition, §8.1 — plus anthropomorphism per Taber's work on chemistry misconceptions):
1. "Potassium is more reactive because it's heavier / hits the water harder."
2. "It's more reactive because it has *more electrons* (more to lose)."
3. "Atoms *want* a full outer shell" — the desire framing that blocks the actual mechanism (attraction/distance).
4. "The metal dissolves" (change of state) rather than *reacts* — listen for this resurfacing when they recall last lesson.

**Core causal chain (the whole lesson in one line):**
*bigger atom → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker attraction → electron lost more easily → more reactive.*

## 0:00–0:02 — Entry

Do Now on desks as they enter. At the door, formal register, twelve words: *"Good morning. Sit down, pens out, Do Now, in silence. Six minutes."*

**Design decisions.** Economy of language and routine (Lemov, §3.1); thinking starts at minute 0 (Willingham, §2.3). With a weak class the *predictability* of the routine matters even more — novelty is load (Sweller, §2.2).

---

## 0:02–0:10 — Retrieval Do Now (printed slip, big font, 6 items — half prerequisites, half last lesson)

**Atomic structure (today's building blocks):**
1. Label the diagram: **nucleus** / **electron** / **shell**. *(word bank provided — matching, not blank-page recall)*
2. Sodium's electronic structure is 2,8,1. Circle the **outer** electron on the diagram.
3. The nucleus has a **positive / negative** charge. *(circle one)*
4. Metals react by **losing / gaining** electrons. *(circle one)*

**Last lesson (the observations today will explain):**
5. Which metal was the MOST violent in water: **lithium / sodium / potassium**? *(circle one)*
6. The gas produced was ________. The water turned purple because an ________ was made. *(word bank: hydrogen · alkali)*

**Check (0:08–0:10):** cold call, question first, 3-second wait; Q3 and Q4 chorally repeated by the whole class ("Metals react by… everyone: *losing electrons*"). Any wrong answer gets a warm correction and a return: *"Hold on — I'll come back to you to say the full answer."* And you do.

**Design decisions**
- **Every item is a prerequisite for today, not general revision.** Today's explanation is *built from* nucleus-charge + outer-electron + losing-electrons, and it explains *last lesson's pattern* — so both must be retrievable before the mechanism has anywhere to attach (Shimamura's Relate, §2.5).
- **Q5–6 are spaced retrieval of last lesson** — the testing effect applied exactly where it pays most (Roediger & Karpicke, §2.6): the observations were encoded once; retrieving them a day later, from memory, before re-exposure, is what makes them stick (Cepeda spacing, §8.6). Do NOT re-show the demo video first — that converts retrieval into recognition and wastes the effect.
- **Recognition formats (circle / match / fill with word bank) instead of free recall.** For a very weak class the Do Now must run at ~90% success (Rosenshine principle 7, §2.1) or it teaches "I can't do science" in the first five minutes. Difficulty is raised *within* the lesson, not at the door.
- **Choral response for the two facts the lesson leans on hardest** (nucleus positive; metals lose electrons): Engelmann's high-frequency automaticity tool (§7.2) — response-per-minute far above hand-raising, zero social risk.
- **"I'll come back to you" — and returning — is Lemov's *No Opt Out* (§5.3):** the sequence ends with the weakest student succeeding out loud.

## 0:10–0:15 — Rebuild last lesson's pattern from memory, then frame the puzzle

1. **Whiteboards (60 s):** *"From memory — no books: write the three metals in order, calmest to most violent. 3-2-1 show me."* Scan. Then rebuild the results table **live under the visualiser from cold-called student memories** — three rows (Li / Na / K), their own observation words from last lesson ("fizzed", "whizzed round", "purple flame").
2. **Frame:**
   > "So this is what you already KNOW: down the group — more violent. Every one of you just showed me that. Here's what nobody in this room can do yet: say **WHY**. And 'why' is the 4-mark question on your exam paper. By the end of today, all of you will be able to write those 4 marks. That's the whole lesson."
3. **Harvest their current theories (whiteboards, one word):** *"Right now, before I teach you anything — WHY do you think potassium goes wildest? One word or a short phrase. No wrong answers yet — I want your honest guess."* Expect *heavier*, *stronger*, *more electrons*, *wants to react*. Say only: *"Keep those in your head. By 10 o'clock you'll know which of them — if any — is right."*

**Design decisions**
- **Students rebuild the table; the teacher doesn't re-present it.** Generation beats re-exposure (Shimamura's G, §2.5; Karpicke & Blunt, §2.6) — and the rebuilt table, in their own words, persisting under the visualiser (Boxer, §6.1), becomes the left-hand column of today's explanation. This is the observation lesson doing its Nuthall-encounter-two job (§7.1).
- **The known-to-unknown framing is Willingham's curiosity gap** (§2.3): "you know WHAT, you can't say WHY" is a moderate-difficulty problem by construction — the answer is one step beyond what they own.
- **The explicit "all of you will get these 4 marks" line is a motivation move for a low-confidence class** (Mccrea, §3.2): weak Foundation classes have usually *practised failure* on "explain" questions; naming the prize as attainable and near-term reframes it.
- **Harvesting theories before teaching is the elicitation step of conceptual change** (Driver §8.1; Posner et al.): the wrong reasons collected here are voted on *by name* at the hinge (0:28) — students confront their own earlier theory, not a strawman. "No wrong answers *yet*" keeps the culture of error intact (Lemov §7.3) while signalling that evidence will adjudicate.
- **No demo re-run, no video montage.** They've seen it; re-showing would be re-exposure (weaker than retrieval) plus seductive-detail risk (Garner/Rey §3.2) — they'd re-watch the flames and stop thinking about electrons. The freed ten minutes is spent at 0:31–0:50 where it buys marks.

## 0:15–0:28 — Explain: the mechanism, drawn live, one element at a time

The heart of the lesson — now with room to breathe. Under the visualiser, plain paper, thick pen. **Slow down; drop volume; this is the 4-mark bit** (register shift — Lemov §3.1).

1. Draw **lithium**: small circle nucleus marked **+**, two small shells, ONE outer electron marked **−**. *"Whiteboards: what charge is the nucleus? Show me. What charge is the electron? Show me."*
2. Draw an arrow between them: *"Positive and negative… everyone: ATTRACT. The nucleus is HOLDING ONTO that outer electron. Like this —"* **magnet and paperclip, live under the camera**: paperclip close to the magnet — *"try to pull it away — hard."*
3. Draw **sodium** next to it: same + nucleus, THREE shells. *"Same single outer electron — that's why it's in the same family. But look — one shell further out."* Magnet: paperclip a little further — weaker grip.
4. Draw **potassium**: FOUR shells — outer electron visibly far away. Magnet: paperclip held far away — barely held, falls. *"Whiteboards: which atom has the WEAKEST grip on its outer electron? Show me."*
5. Cold call the chain, one link per student, 3-second waits: *"Bigger atom, so the outer electron is… (further from the nucleus). So the attraction is… (weaker). So the electron is lost… (more easily). And a metal that loses its electron easily is MORE… (reactive)."* Then point back at the results table from 0:10: *"And THAT is why the potassium you watched last week went up in flames and the lithium just fizzed."*
6. Write the chain as **five boxes with arrows** under the drawings — students copy ONLY this chain (one minute, five short phrases):
   **bigger atom → outer electron further from nucleus → weaker attraction → electron lost more easily → MORE reactive**
7. Kill the "wants" framing explicitly: *"Notice what I did NOT say. I didn't say potassium WANTS to lose its electron. Atoms don't want anything — they're not alive. It's just a weaker grip. Weaker grip, electron lost, job done. If you write 'wants a full shell' in the exam, you get zero for that line."*
8. Choral decode the two polysyllables they must write today: *at-TRAC-tion, re-AC-tive* — my turn, your turn.

**Design decisions**
- **Live drawing, not a finished slide diagram**: segmenting, signalling, contiguity (Mayer §6.3); one element at a time is the only pace a weak class's working memory can follow (Sweller §2.2); the drawing persists as the reference for everything after (Boxer §6.1). With the demo phase gone, there's now time to draw **all three** atoms — Li/Na/K side by side is a minimally-different **example sequence** (Engelmann §2.4): the ONLY visible difference is distance, so distance is the only available explanation. Three data points also mirror the three rows of their own results table — the two representations line up row for row (dual coding, Paivio/Caviglioli §6.3).
- **Step 5's explicit link back to their own observations** is the moment the mechanism explains *their* data, not the textbook's — Willingham's causal story structure (§2.3) closing its loop, and encounter two being *connected* to encounter one rather than floating free (Nuthall §7.1).
- **The magnet-paperclip analogy is concrete, physical, and shown — not described.** Dual coding plus a physical referent for the abstract word *attraction*. State its limit in one line ("electrons aren't paperclips — but the grip idea is right") — analogies unpoliced become next year's misconceptions (Driver §8.1).
- **The causal chain is the only thing copied all lesson**, and it's copied *after* being built orally — generation before transcription (Shimamura §2.5). Five boxes ≈ the exam mark scheme's creditable steps: the note-form IS the answer plan.
- **Anthropomorphism is refuted by name** ("atoms don't want anything") — the refutation approach (Tippett §8.1); Taber's chemistry-misconception research shows the full-shell "desire" framework actively blocks the attraction mechanism, so leaving it standing costs the 4 marks.
- **Question every ~90 seconds, all cold call + choral + whiteboards, ~90% success** — Rosenshine 3, 6, 7 tuned for this class (§5.1, and the Foundation preamble above).

## 0:28–0:31 — Hinge question (decision point)

Boards, A/B/C/D, 30 seconds, simultaneous reveal:

> **Why is potassium more reactive than lithium?**
> **A.** Potassium atoms are heavier, so they hit the water harder
> **B.** Potassium has more electrons, so it has more to lose
> **C.** Potassium's outer electron is further from the nucleus, so it is lost more easily
> **D.** Potassium wants a full outer shell more than lithium does

Every distractor is one of the theories harvested at 0:14. **Decision rules:** ≥80% C → proceed. 30–70% → 90-second convince-your-neighbour, revote (Mazur §8.3). <30% → redraw lithium and potassium with ONLY the distance difference highlighted, re-chain chorally, re-ask.

Land it: *"Fifteen minutes ago some of you honestly guessed A, B and D. That's what scientists do — guess, then test the guess against the evidence. The evidence says C."*

**Design decisions.** Wiliam's hinge design (§5.2): all answer in under a minute, wrong answers are *diagnoses* not noise, and the responsive move is planned before the lesson, not improvised (§7.4). The distractors are the students' *own* pre-instruction theories — voting against your own earlier idea, with evidence, is the conceptual-change moment (Posner et al. §8.1) made visible and made safe (culture of error, §7.3).

## 0:31–0:50 — Guided → faded practice: writing the 4-marker (the reinvested demo time)

1. **I do (4 min), visualiser:** the actual AQA-style question — *"Explain why potassium is more reactive than lithium. (4)"* Teacher writes a full answer live, narrating: *"I don't start writing. I start by pointing at my chain. Every box = a sentence. Watch: box one…"* Four short sentences appear, each tied to a box.
2. **We do (4 min):** same question, new pair — *sodium vs lithium* — answer built from cold-called sentences, teacher scribing under the camera.
3. **Faded (7 min):** students write their own for *"Explain why sodium is MORE reactive than lithium"* with the **sentence frame** on the board:
   - *As you go down Group 1, the atoms get ________.*
   - *The outer electron is ________ from the nucleus.*
   - *So the attraction between the nucleus and the outer electron is ________.*
   - *So the outer electron is lost more ________, which makes the metal ________ reactive.*
   Word bank: *bigger · further · weaker · easily · more.*
4. **Second faded question (4 min) — the extra rep the demo time bought:** *"Rubidium is below potassium in Group 1. Explain why rubidium is even MORE reactive than potassium."* Frame still visible but students are told: *"Try it without looking at the frame first. Peek only if stuck."* **Stretch (do NOT gate on it):** rewrite with no frame; word equation with scaffolding — *rubidium + water → rubidium ________ + hydrogen.*
5. Circulate hunting ONE pre-decided error: answers that describe ("it fizzes more") instead of explain (no *attraction* link). At ~0:46, **show-call** one strong answer: *"Count the boxes in Jordan's answer — all four. That's the 4 marks."* Two minutes: everyone checks their own against the chain, adds any missing box.

**Design decisions**
- **Worked example → completion → frame → frame-optional is the full fading ladder** (Sweller & Cooper; Renkl & Atkinson, §6.2) — and cutting the demo is what makes the fourth rung possible in one lesson. For a very weak class, the frame is the difference between practising the science and staring at a blank line; the *"peek only if stuck"* instruction on rep two is the fade in action — expertise reversal (§2.2) says scaffolds must be withdrawn as competence grows, and rep two is where today's competence exists.
- **The rubidium question is transfer, not repetition**: it forces the chain to run on a metal they've *never seen*, which is the actual AQA Foundation skill (*predict/explain from a given trend*) and the test of whether the pattern generalised beyond the three metals in the table.
- **The chain-to-sentences routine is metacognitive modelling** (Collins et al., §6.1): what step 1 actually teaches is *how an expert converts a diagram into exam prose* — the transferable skill this class lacks most.
- **Word equations remain stretch, not core — deliberate curriculum triage.** Engelmann's mastery-before-movement (§2.4): one secure causal chain beats two shaky objectives; the reactions' equations get consolidated next lesson.
- **Show-call counts boxes, publicly and warmly** (Lemov §6.4; Sadler/Christodoulou §6.4): quality is *shown* as a checkable structure, not described in rubric-speak — and self-checking against the chain makes the feedback more work for the recipient than the donor (Wiliam §7.5). No grades on any of it (Butler 1988, §7.5).

## 0:50–0:55 — Exit ticket (slips, silent, collected)

1. Circle: as you go DOWN Group 1, reactivity gets **higher / lower**.
2. **Explain why** in one or two sentences. You must use the words **outer electron**, **nucleus**, **attraction**. *(no frame on the slip; frame available on request — note who asks)*
3. Caesium is at the very BOTTOM of Group 1. A student says: *"Caesium is the most reactive because its atoms are the heaviest."* What is **wrong** with their reason, and what is the **right** reason?

**Tonight (10 min):** sort into three piles — *secure / describes-but-no-mechanism / heavier- or "wants"-misconception intact*. Piles write tomorrow's Do Now and decide whether you re-teach the chain for 3 minutes. The chain is scheduled to reappear in next week's spaced starter and again at Group 7 (where the logic runs in reverse — gaining electrons — so today's chain is the anchor).

**Design decisions.** Q2 without the frame is the honest check — did the frame scaffold learning or replace it? (Whoever asks for it is tomorrow's target group.) Q3 is a **refutation task the student performs** (Tippett §8.1) aimed squarely at the most popular harvested misconception, and doubles as the trend-extrapolation skill. The three-pile sort is Wiliam's read-then-act rule (§7.4); the planned reappearances are Nuthall's three-encounters finding (§7.1) — today was encounter two; the sort decides how encounter three opens.

---

## 0:55–0:60 — Student-generated close

*"Books closed. Whiteboards. Draw my three atoms — small, medium, big — and the five boxes. From memory. Two minutes. Go."* Boards up; compare against the visualiser drawing (put it back under the camera); choral run-through of the chain one final time; preview: *"Next — the OPPOSITE family. Group 7. Where the trend flips upside-down, and you already own the reason."*

**Design decisions.** Free-recall brain dump of the *diagram + chain* is generation in both codes — verbal and visual (Dunlosky's practice testing, §8.6; Paivio §6.3; Shimamura's Generate/Evaluate §2.5). The teacher recapping instead would be the teacher doing the remembering (§2.6). Ending by previewing Group 7 as "the same reason, flipped" plants the relational link that turns two topics into one schema (Rosenshine principle 10, §2.1).

## Question budget (as planned)

| Phase | Written/all-student | Oral (cold call/choral) |
|---|---|---|
| Do Now + check | 6 | ~8 (incl. 2 choral) |
| Rebuild pattern + frame + harvest | 3 (whiteboard) | ~5 |
| Explain (live drawing) | 4 (whiteboard) | ~10 (incl. chorals) |
| Hinge | 1 (MCQ) | ~3 |
| Guided/faded writing | 2 extended + frame | ~9 |
| Exit + close | 3 + brain dump | ~4 |
| **Total** | **~19 all-student responses** | **~40+ oral** |

That oral count sits at the effective-teacher end of Evertson et al.'s distribution (§5.1); for a weak class the balance deliberately shifts toward **whiteboard + choral** (zero-writing-cost, zero-social-risk responses) — except the two 4-markers, which get nineteen fully scaffolded minutes.

## Deliberately left out

- **Re-running or re-showing the demo/video** — they saw it last lesson; retrieval of it beats re-exposure to it (Roediger & Karpicke §2.6), and a replay is seductive detail (Garner/Rey §3.2): they'd remember the flames again instead of the electrons. The observations enter today's lesson through *their memory*, on whiteboards, at 0:02 and 0:10.
- **Shielding** — Chemistry-only; adds load, earns no Trilogy marks (curriculum triage, Engelmann §2.4).
- **Balanced symbol equations** — next lesson, after the trend explanation is secure (mastery before movement).
- **Copying objectives, "any questions?", hands-up, grades** — the standing pitfalls (§10).
