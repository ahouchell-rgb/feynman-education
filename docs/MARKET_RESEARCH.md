# EdTech Market Research: Finding Defensible White Space

**Prepared:** June 2026
**Brief:** Where can a GCSE/A-Level teacher who can code (or hire a dev) build an education app with a credible path to ≥ a few £k/month MRR, ideally much more — avoiding saturated niches like driving-theory and generic revision?
**Method:** Multi-source web research with 3-vote adversarial fact-checking (109 research/verification agents, 26 sources fetched, 55 claims extracted, 25 verified, 21 confirmed, 4 explicitly refuted). Confidence levels and refuted claims are flagged throughout so you can trust what survived and discard what didn't.

> **Honest headline:** The most *obvious* AI-enabled openings (speaking-exam practice, exam-board-benchmarked marking) are **already being entered by funded products**. Durable white space is narrowing fast. The realistic edge is **not** "an AI app for X" in the abstract — it's a **narrow, board-specific, distribution-led** product where you out-execute on calibration, a specific underserved sub-segment, or a specific buyer who already pays. This report shows where those pockets are.

## 1. Market size & structural headroom

| Metric | Figure | Source | Confidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| UK EdTech market size (2025) | ~**USD 9.8B** | IMARC | Medium (vendor estimate) |
| UK EdTech CAGR (2026–34) | ~**19.7%** (high-end outlier) | IMARC | Medium — Grand View ~13%, MarketDataForecast ~15.8% are lower |
| Global EdTech spend (2025) | ~**$404B**, 16.3% CAGR (2.5× since 2019) | HolonIQ | Medium (a ~2020 forecast; newer estimates ~$187–277B) |
| **Digital as share of total education spend** | only **~5.2%** of a **$7.3T** global market | HolonIQ | **High** |

**What actually matters here:** ignore the headline billions (they're vendor estimates with opaque methodology — IMARC's own "$52B UK by 2034" endpoint was **refuted** in our checks). The one figure that holds up and *is* strategically useful is the **5.2%**: education spend is overwhelmingly still non-digital. There is real structural headroom — but headroom ≠ easy money, because the *consumer* exam-prep slice specifically is crowded and price-compressed (see §2).

**Where the money actually concentrates (verified):**
- **29%** of UK 11–16s have had private tutoring (up from 18% twenty years ago), **peaking at Year 11 — the GCSE year — at 25%**, then Year 10 at 10%. *(Sutton Trust, Private Tutoring 2026 — high confidence.)* Translation: **parent spend clusters hard around the GCSE exam year and 11+ entrance.** Build for the payer, at the moment they're scared.

## 2. The red ocean — niches to AVOID

These are saturated, often by **free** or near-free incumbents. Competing on the same axis is a losing game.

| Niche | Dominant incumbent(s) | Why it's a trap |
|---|---|---|
| **Generic GCSE/A-Level revision content** | **Physics & Maths Tutor** (free past papers, notes, mark schemes, flashcards — no sign-up); **Save My Exams** (freemium, ~**£4/mo** annual); **Seneca** (~14M students, used by ~90% of English secondaries per its own data; acquired by GoStudent) | A large *free* incumbent already covers exactly what paid apps charge for. Content is commoditised. **High confidence.** |
| **B2B school revision platforms** | **GCSEPod** — 1,700+ secondary schools, **1 in 2 UK academies** (Access Group) | ~40–50% secondary penetration; deeply embedded, sold through MAT procurement. **Medium confidence.** |
| **Generic flashcards / spaced repetition** | Quizlet, Anki | Zero differentiation possible; free and entrenched. |
| **Mass-market language learning** | Duolingo | Capital- and brand-dominated; do not enter. |
| **Driving theory** | (your original idea) | Confirmed saturated — correct call to leave. |
| **11+/entrance prep (B2C)** | **Atom Learning** — **£40–£70/mo**, 75,000+ children, 1,000+ schools, **$25M SoftBank Series A** | High *willingness-to-pay* (parents pay £70/mo!) BUT a deep-pocketed incumbent owns it. Enter only on a wedge it ignores. **High confidence.** |

**The pattern:** content is free; the saturated layer is "explain the syllabus." Don't sell content. Sell **something adjacent to content** that's hard to commoditise (assessment, feedback, practice of a *skill*, or access to a *buyer*).

## 3. Where the white space actually is

The defensible openings sit **adjacent to content, not in it** — and ideally where (a) a specific *payer* already spends, and (b) the work is a *skill to be practised/marked*, not a fact to be looked up.

### 3a. Confirmed-viable categories (but already being entered — you need a sub-wedge)

- **Oral / speaking-exam practice.** **Marvely** does GCSE MFL (French/German/Spanish) speaking, board-specific (AQA, Edexcel), AI role-plays with mark-scheme feedback — **£7.99/mo individual, £199.99 whole-school** (launch price). *High confidence this is viable; it's also occupied for MFL GCSE.* **The wedge:** the sub-segments Marvely doesn't own yet — **A-Level MFL speaking**, **GCSE English spoken-language endorsement**, **MFL beyond the big three** (e.g. Mandarin, Arabic, Urdu for GCSE), or **professional spoken assessments** (OET/IELTS speaking, nursing OSCEs).
- **AI marking benchmarked to mark schemes.** **Top Marks AI** (B2B, schools/MATs, 400+ tools, 40+ subjects, AQA/Edexcel/OCR/Eduqas/WJEC/CCEA/CIE) and **MarkMe** (consumer, instant GCSE/A-Level essay marking mapped to AO1–AO3, freemium one-mark/week). *High confidence the category works; it's contested.*
  - ⚠️ **Two moat claims here were explicitly REFUTED:** Top Marks' "90%+ correlation vs ~65% human" accuracy stat (0–3) and MarkMe's "fine-tuned-on-real-GCSE-answers moat" (0–3). **Do not believe that fine-tuning on exam answers is a defensible moat — it isn't.** The real moat is distribution (school contracts), mark-scheme calibration data, and trust — not the model.

### 3b. Genuinely thinner-occupied pockets (where no funded incumbent yet dominates)

These came through as *signals* (lower verification weight than §3a, treat as leads to validate, not proven facts):

- **SEND / neurodiverse learners.** ~1 in 7 UK learners are neurodiverse, yet revision/assessment tools assume a neurotypical, timed-exam workflow. Almost nobody builds *for* dyslexia/ADHD/dyspraxia first. Underserved and sympathetic buyers (parents, SENCos with budgets).
- **A-Level subjects with no dedicated app** — the long tail incumbents skip because the cohorts are "too small": A-Level Psychology, Sociology, Economics, Politics, Business, RS, Geography, History essay technique. Small per-subject TAM but near-zero competition and high exam-technique pain.
- **Coursework / NEA support** — EPQ, A-Level NEAs, GCSE English/Geography/History coursework. High-anxiety, high-value, under-tooled; AI feedback-on-drafts fits perfectly and incumbents avoid it (marking-integrity nervousness).
- **Exam *technique* vs content** — "how to get the marks" (command words, AO weighting, mark-scheme decode) rather than "what's the answer." Different axis from PMT/Seneca.
- **Professional / vocational certification** — separate, higher-WTP market. **Achievable** (US FINRA securities SIE/Series 7, NASAA 63/65/66, GRE/USMLE/ACT) and **Pocket Prep** (100+ certifications) prove adults pay well for career exams. *Verified the model works; the specific "$200–400 Series 7" price point was **refuted**, so don't quote it.* **UK angle is wide open:** ACCA/AAT, CeMAP, financial-advice exams, NEBOSH, CIPD, gas/electrical certs, nursing revalidation.

## 4. Where AI genuinely changed what's buildable

LLMs made three things newly viable that weren't 2–3 years ago:
1. **Open-response marking** against a rubric/mark scheme (essays, long-answer science).
2. **Socratic tutoring** that doesn't just give the answer.
3. **Personalised question generation** at scale.

**But:** content-generation is the *most crowded* AI-EdTech category. The white space is **not** "AI generates X" — it's AI applied to an **assessment/feedback** task, in a **specific board/subject**, sold to a **specific buyer**. And remember the refuted moat claims: a generic LLM wrapper is not defensible. Defensibility comes from **calibration data + distribution + trust**, not the model.

## 5. Willingness-to-pay & monetisation benchmarks (verified, high confidence)

From RevenueCat *State of Subscription Apps 2025* (75,000+ apps):
- **Education app median revenue per install ≈ $0.27** (below the $0.31 cross-category median; P90 $3.13). Education monetises *thinly per install* — you need either volume or a high-value niche.
- **Annual billing dominates**: Education annual plan **$43.94** vs monthly **$8.13** (5.4× revenue skew). **Push annual plans.**
- **Free→paid conversion ~2%** at Day-35 in Western Europe (2.6% North America, 1.4% IN/SEA) — *app-wide median, not education-specific*, so treat as a ceiling-ish anchor.

**Implications for your model:**
- B2C consumer exam apps are **price-compressed** (~£4–8/mo) by free incumbents. To hit a few £k/month you need thousands of payers OR a premium niche.
- **B2B (schools/MATs)** and **high-WTP segments** (11+ at £40–70/mo; professional certs) are where the *fast* path to a few £k/month lives — fewer customers, bigger cheques. One MAT contract can beat a thousand £4 subscribers.

## 6. Ranked product ideas — fastest credible path to a few £k/month first

> Ranked by *speed-to-revenue × defensibility ÷ build difficulty* for a coding teacher. £-estimates are planning scenarios, not forecasts.

### #1 — AI marking + exam-technique tool for ONE underserved A-Level essay subject, sold B2B to departments
- **Gap:** Top Marks/MarkMe go broad; no one owns *deep* marking + technique coaching for a specific essay-heavy A-Level (Psychology, Sociology, History, RS, Politics, Business).
- **Customer:** Heads of Department (they have small CPD/resource budgets and feel the marking-workload pain acutely). Sell per-department, not per-pupil.
- **Why incumbents miss it:** breadth-first products are shallow per subject; departments want their *exact* board's mark scheme nailed.
- **Model:** £300–800/dept/year B2B, or £6.99/mo B2C add-on. **10–15 departments ≈ a few £k/month.**
- **Build:** Medium. LLM + rubric calibration + your own marked-answer exemplars (your moat as a teacher).
- **Moat:** your subject expertise + calibrated exemplars + department relationships. **Risk:** marking-accuracy trust; mitigate with "AI-assisted, teacher-reviewed" framing.

### #2 — Speaking-exam practice for an *unoccupied* sub-segment
- **Gap:** Marvely owns GCSE MFL (big three). **A-Level MFL speaking, GCSE English spoken endorsement, or community-language GCSEs (Mandarin/Arabic/Urdu/Polish)** are open.
- **Customer:** students + parents (B2C £7.99/mo) and whole-school (£200+/yr) — model proven by Marvely.
- **Why incumbents miss it:** smaller cohorts per language; they chase the big three first.
- **Build:** Medium–high (speech-to-text + LLM + mark-scheme feedback). **Moat:** board-specific content + being first in that language/tier.
- **Risk:** speech recognition quality for non-English; cohort size per language.

### #3 — UK professional/vocational certification prep (the Achievable/Pocket Prep model, UK-localised)
- **Gap:** US has Achievable/Pocket Prep; **UK professional exams are under-apped** — CeMAP, AAT, ACCA papers, NEBOSH, CIPD, financial-advice/regulatory exams.
- **Customer:** working adults (highest WTP; career ROI justifies £15–40/mo or £100–300 one-off).
- **Why it's attractive:** adults pay far more than students; B2B2C via employers/training providers; **lowest content-refresh burden** if you pick a stable syllabus.
- **Build:** Low–medium (question bank + spaced practice + explanations). **Moat:** licensed/curated question bank + pass-rate reputation. **Risk:** you're outside your GCSE subject expertise — pick a cert you can credibly author or partner on.

### #4 — SEND/neurodiverse-first revision & assessment tool
- **Gap:** ~1 in 7 learners neurodiverse; tools assume neurotypical timed workflows. Almost no one builds dyslexia/ADHD-first.
- **Customer:** parents of SEND students + SENCos (ring-fenced budgets, EHCP funding).
- **Why incumbents miss it:** accessibility is an afterthought feature, not a product thesis.
- **Build:** Medium. **Moat:** genuine accessibility depth + SENCo trust. **Risk:** smaller TAM, longer trust-building; strong mission/PR upside.

### #5 — Coursework/NEA/EPQ AI feedback coach
- **Gap:** EPQ + A-Level NEAs + GCSE coursework are high-anxiety, under-tooled; incumbents avoid (integrity nerves).
- **Customer:** students (B2C) and schools (B2B), seasonal spikes.
- **Why incumbents miss it:** academic-integrity caution. **Position as "feedback on *your* draft," not "writes it for you."**
- **Build:** Medium. **Moat:** rubric alignment + teacher framing. **Risk:** misuse/integrity perception — design guardrails in from day one.

### #6 — 11+ wedge against Atom (only on what Atom ignores)
- **Gap:** Atom is comprehensive/expensive (£40–70/mo). Wedges: specific independent-school **Common Entrance / pre-test** styles, **regional grammar consortia** (CEM/GL variants), or a **cheaper mock-only** tier.
- **Customer:** anxious parents (proven £40–70/mo WTP).
- **Build:** Medium. **Moat:** hyper-specific exam-format fidelity. **Risk:** competing near a $25M-funded incumbent — stay in the cracks it won't bother with.

---

## 7. Recommended next move

1. **Pick ONE of #1–#3.** For *fastest* revenue with *your* GCSE/A-Level credibility, **#1 (deep AI marking + technique for one essay A-Level, sold to departments)** is the strongest fit: B2B cheques, your subject expertise as the moat, build difficulty medium, and it sidesteps the price-compressed B2C content ocean.
2. **Validate before building:** email/DM 15–20 heads of department for that subject; pre-sell. If 5 say "take my money," build. (Note: this repo already contains a secondary-ed product with marking, mastery-graph, parent-report and SLT-dashboard scaffolding — #1 and #5 are the closest fit to what's here.)
3. **Monetise annual, B2B-first.** A handful of department/school contracts hits "a few £k/month" faster than thousands of £4 consumer subs.
4. **Don't claim an AI moat you don't have.** Lead with calibration, trust, and distribution.

## Appendix C — Caveats

- All market-size figures are **commercial vendor estimates** with opaque methodology — attributed data points, not ground truth. IMARC's CAGR is a high-end outlier; HolonIQ's $404B looks high vs newer ~$187–277B 2025 estimates.
- Several competitor facts rest on **marketing/self-reported pages** (PMT, Save My Exams, GCSEPod, Atom self-reported scale). Many primary URLs returned HTTP 403, so verification leaned on search-indexed snippets.
- RevenueCat Day-35 conversion is **app-wide median**, not education-specific.
- Pricing and feature sets (Marvely £199.99 launch price, Atom tiers) are **2025–26 snapshots** and change frequently.
- **Most important strategic caveat:** the most attractive AI-adjacent niches (speaking practice, board-benchmarked marking) are **already occupied by funded products**. Verified evidence confirms the *categories* are viable but does **not** establish which specific sub-niches remain unoccupied — that's the validation work in §7.
