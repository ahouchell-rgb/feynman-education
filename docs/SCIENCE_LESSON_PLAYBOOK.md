# The Research-Informed Science Lesson: A Complete Playbook

**How to structure, run, and deliver a science lesson — what the research actually says**

This document synthesises the primary research literature on instruction into a single practitioner-facing guide for planning and delivering a science lesson. It covers lesson architecture, teacher voice and register, classroom talk, questioning (how many, what type, how delivered), visualiser technique, checks for understanding, and science-specific pedagogy (misconceptions, practical work, demonstrations).

Every recommendation is attributed to the named researcher(s) whose work supports it. Where the evidence is correlational, contested, or context-bound, that is stated. This guide deliberately cites primary figures and their original works rather than aggregator organisations.

## 1. The evidence base

The recommendations in this guide rest on four converging bodies of work. When independent research traditions using different methods arrive at the same answer, confidence rises sharply — and on the core of this guide, they do.

**Process–product classroom research (1960s–80s).** Researchers such as Jere Brophy, Thomas Good, Carolyn Evertson, and Barak Rosenshine observed hundreds of classrooms, coded what teachers actually did, and correlated behaviours with student achievement gains. The most effective teachers reviewed prior learning, taught in small steps, asked large numbers of questions, checked all students' understanding, and secured high success rates before moving on. Rosenshine's *Principles of Instruction* (2012) is the canonical distillation.

**Cognitive science of learning and memory.** John Sweller's cognitive load theory, Alan Baddeley's working-memory model, Allan Paivio's dual coding theory, Richard Mayer's multimedia learning research, Henry Roediger and Jeffrey Karpicke's retrieval-practice studies, Robert Bjork's "desirable difficulties," and Daniel Willingham's synthesis in *Why Don't Students Like School?* (2009) explain **why** the process–product findings work: working memory is severely limited, novices and experts learn differently, and memory is strengthened by effortful retrieval, not by re-exposure.

**Formative assessment research.** Paul Black and Dylan Wiliam's *Inside the Black Box* (1998) and Wiliam's *Embedded Formative Assessment* (2011) established that minute-to-minute, in-lesson evidence gathering — followed by responsive teaching — produces some of the largest improvements documented in education research. Graham Nuthall's *The Hidden Lives of Learners* (2007) explains why it is necessary: teachers systematically misperceive what students know.

**Science education research.** Rosalind Driver's misconceptions programme, Ian Abrahams and Robin Millar's analysis of practical work, Richard White and Richard Gunstone's Predict–Observe–Explain, Eric Mazur's Peer Instruction, and the Kirschner–Sweller–Clark critique of minimally guided instruction give science teaching its subject-specific evidence base.

**A note on humility.** Nuthall spent 40 years recording individual students through whole units and concluded that most of what determines learning is invisible from the front of the room. Robert Coe has similarly warned that busy, engaged-looking classrooms are a "poor proxy" for learning. Everything below should therefore be read as *the best-supported bets available*, deployed with constant checking — not as a script that guarantees learning.

## 2. Lesson architecture

### 2.1 Rosenshine's Principles of Instruction — the spine of the lesson

Barak Rosenshine (2012, *American Educator*) synthesised three evidence streams — cognitive science, studies of master teachers, and cognitive-supports research — into ten principles. They are the most defensible default structure for a science lesson:

1. **Begin with a short review of previous learning** (5–8 minutes).
2. **Present new material in small steps**, with student practice after each step.
3. **Ask a large number of questions** and check the responses of all students.
4. **Provide models** — worked examples, thinking aloud, demonstrations.
5. **Guide student practice** — more time restating, elaborating, and rehearsing than most teachers allow.
6. **Check for understanding** at each point, rather than asking "are there any questions?"
7. **Obtain a high success rate.** Rosenshine cites an optimal success rate of roughly 80% during guided practice — high enough to show learning is happening, low enough to show material is challenging.
8. **Provide scaffolds for difficult tasks** — and fade them.
9. **Require and monitor independent practice**, to the point of fluency ("overlearning").
10. **Engage students in weekly and monthly review**, because spaced re-exposure builds the connected schemata that make recall automatic.

Tom Sherrington's *Rosenshine's Principles in Action* (2019) usefully groups these into four strands — **sequencing concepts and modelling, questioning, reviewing material, and stages of practice** — which map directly onto lesson phases.

**Default phase structure for a science lesson (derived from Rosenshine):**

| Phase | Approx. share of a 60-min lesson | Principles in play |
|---|---|---|
| Retrieval starter / review of prior learning | 8–10 min | 1, 10 |
| New material in small steps + modelling | 15–20 min, chunked, never one continuous block | 2, 4 |
| Guided practice with heavy questioning | 15–20 min | 3, 5, 6, 7 |
| Independent practice | 10–15 min | 8, 9 |
| Final check / exit ticket | 3–5 min | 6 |

The critical structural insight is **alternation**: not I-talk-then-you-work, but short input → question burst → short practice → check → next step. Rosenshine reports that the most effective teachers spent *more* time (roughly 23 vs. 11 minutes in one comparison) on presentation and guided practice than less effective teachers — because they invested in checking and rehearsal *before* releasing students to independent work, where errors would otherwise be practised and consolidated.

### 2.2 Cognitive load theory (Sweller) — why small steps

John Sweller's cognitive load theory (Sweller 1988; Sweller, Ayres & Kalyuga 2011) rests on working memory's tiny capacity for novel information (Miller's 7±2; Cowan's more recent ~4 chunks) versus long-term memory's effective limitlessness. Implications for lesson structure:

- **Element interactivity determines difficulty.** Topics where many elements must be processed simultaneously (e.g., balancing equations, stoichiometry, interpreting rate graphs) demand smaller steps and more worked examples than low-interactivity content (e.g., learning hazard symbols).
- **The worked-example effect** (Sweller & Cooper 1985): novices learn more from studying worked examples than from solving equivalent problems. Alternate example–problem pairs; then **fade** worked examples by progressively deleting steps for the learner to complete (Renkl & Atkinson 2003).
- **The expertise-reversal effect** (Kalyuga et al. 2003): what helps novices (full worked examples, integrated guidance) becomes redundant and even harmful for relative experts. Structure must therefore *change across a unit* — heavy modelling early, problem solving later.
- **Split-attention and redundancy effects**: integrate labels into diagrams rather than placing text beside them; do not read aloud text that students are simultaneously reading (see §6 on Mayer).
- **The transient-information effect**: spoken explanation disappears; long verbal chains overload working memory. Anchor extended explanations to something persistent — a diagram built live under the visualiser (§6).

### 2.3 Willingham — memory is the residue of thought

Daniel Willingham's core dictum (*Why Don't Students Like School?*, 2009): **"Memory is the residue of thought."** Students remember what they think hard about — so the central planning question for every lesson segment is *"what will students actually be thinking about during this activity?"* A spectacular demonstration in which students think mostly about the flames, not the chemistry, produces memories of flames. Willingham's other structural contributions:

- **Story structure ("the privileged status of story").** Organise explanations around conflict, causality, and question — *why* does the pressure rise? *what would happen if…?* — because causal narrative is more memorable than exposition.
- **Curiosity comes from working on problems of moderate difficulty** — questions students can *almost* answer. Open a topic with a puzzle pitched just beyond current knowledge (cf. §8 on Predict–Observe–Explain).
- **Factual knowledge precedes skill.** "Critical thinking" in science is not content-free; analysis of data about, say, enzymes depends on knowledge of enzymes. Do not structure lessons around generic skills practice with thin content.

### 2.4 Engelmann's Direct Instruction — faultless communication

Siegfried Engelmann's Direct Instruction (Engelmann & Carnine, *Theory of Instruction*, 1982) was the standout performer of **Project Follow Through** (1968–77), the largest educational experiment ever conducted in the US, where DI outperformed other models on basic skills, cognitive problem-solving, *and* self-esteem measures. Even if you never use a scripted programme, three DI design principles transfer directly to science teaching:

- **Faultless communication**: design explanations and example sequences so that only one interpretation is logically possible. Most "misconceptions" are rational inferences from ambiguous teaching.
- **Example and non-example sequences**: concepts are taught by minimally different pairs. To teach *element vs. compound*, show sequences that vary one feature at a time; to teach *what counts as a force*, include non-examples students typically miscategorise. (Engelmann: the examples define the concept far more precisely than the definition does.)
- **Mastery before movement**: do not proceed while any component skill is insecure; later learning is built from earlier parts.

### 2.5 Shimamura's MARGE — a whole-lesson attention model

Arthur Shimamura — a memory neuroscientist — offered MARGE (*MARGE: A Whole-Brain Learning Approach*, 2018) as a free synthesis for teachers:

- **M — Motivate**: activate top-down attention by framing the big question; curiosity gates encoding.
- **A — Attend**: attention is the bottleneck; one focus at a time; the "3 Cs" — categorise, compare, contrast.
- **R — Relate**: new information must connect to existing knowledge; elaborative interrogation ("why would that be true?").
- **G — Generate**: recall and restating in one's own words beats re-reading — "the production effect"; students should *generate* the material (self-explanation, teach-back, retrieval).
- **E — Evaluate**: metacognitive checking — quizzes, self-testing, spaced review.

MARGE and Rosenshine are complementary: Rosenshine gives the phase order; MARGE reminds you that each phase must recruit motivation, attention, and generation, not just exposure.

### 2.6 Openings and closings

- **Open with retrieval, not administration.** Roediger & Karpicke (2006) showed that retrieving information strengthens memory far more than restudying it (the *testing effect*); Karpicke & Blunt (2011, *Science*) showed retrieval practice beat even concept mapping for science text learning. A 4–6 question "Do Now" reviewing last lesson / last week / last term operationalises Rosenshine's principles 1 and 10 and Bjork's spacing. Pooja Agarwal and Patrice Bain (*Powerful Teaching*, 2019) recommend low-stakes ("no-stakes") retrieval as routine, with feedback given immediately after.
- **Space and interleave the starter's content.** Cepeda et al. (2006, 2008) established that spaced re-study substantially outperforms massed re-study, with the optimal gap growing as the retention interval grows. A common practitioner scheme (e.g., Kate Jones, *Retrieval Practice*, 2019) is "last lesson / last week / last topic / way back."
- **Close with an exit check, not a recap monologue.** Wiliam (2011) frames the exit ticket as evidence for *planning the next lesson*; the value is in what the teacher reads afterwards, not the ritual. A teacher summary at lesson end is the *teacher* doing the generation — flip it so students generate (a brain dump, a one-sentence causal summary, a hinge question).

## 3. Voice, tone, and register

The research on teacher voice divides into (a) behavioural technique — what effective teachers do with volume, pace, and register, best codified by Doug Lemov; (b) the psychology of teacher enthusiasm and prosody; and (c) occupational voice health.

### 3.1 Lemov's "Strong Voice" — the mechanics of authority without shouting

Doug Lemov's *Teach Like a Champion* (3.0, 2021) — built from systematic video study of high-performing teachers — codifies five techniques under **Strong Voice**:

1. **Economy of language.** When giving directions, fewer words = more compliance. Strip qualifiers and chatter: "Pens down. Eyes on me." Excess words dilute the signal and load working memory.
2. **Do not talk over.** Wait for full attention before instruction that matters; use the *self-interrupt* — stop mid-word ("Turn to pa—") and resume only when silent — which asserts that your words matter without conflict.
3. **Do not engage.** Stay on your topic; don't be pulled into side-arguments when redirecting behaviour.
4. **Square up / stand still.** When giving a direction, stop moving, turn your whole body to the class; movement while directing signals the direction is unimportant.
5. **Quiet power.** Counter-intuitively, when you most want control, **drop volume and slow down**. Getting louder signals nervousness; getting quieter and slower signals composure and makes students lean in.

**Register.** Lemov distinguishes casual, formal, and urgent registers. Default teaching lives in the *formal register* — controlled, warm, deliberate; the *urgent register* is reserved and rare (its power comes from contrast); casual register signals "this is conversation, not instruction." Deliberately *shifting* register is the tool: dropping into slow, quiet formality marks "this is the important bit."

### 3.2 Enthusiasm and prosody — tone carries information

- **Teacher enthusiasm is reliably associated with student interest, intrinsic motivation, and attention.** Melanie Keller, Thomas Goetz and colleagues (Keller, Hoy, Goetz & Frenzel 2016, *Educational Psychology Review*) reviewed the construct: displayed enthusiasm (expressive gesture, vocal variation, energy) predicts student motivation and enjoyment, partly via emotional contagion. Patricia Kelly and colleagues, and earlier Barak Rosenshine (1970 review of enthusiasm studies), found enthusiasm one of the more consistent correlates of achievement among "style" variables.
- **Vocal variation is an attention technology.** Monotone delivery flattens the salience cues listeners use to segment speech; varying pitch, pace, and volume marks what matters (this is the prosodic analogue of Mayer's *signalling principle*, §6.3). Slow down and lower pitch at definitions and key causal links; speed up through low-stakes connective tissue.
- **The "seductive detail" warning applies to performance too** (Garner, Gillingham & White 1989; Rey 2012 review): jokes, anecdotes, and drama that are *interesting but irrelevant* to the target content measurably reduce learning of that content. Enthusiasm should energise *the science*, not compete with it — Willingham's residue-of-thought rule again.
- **Peps Mccrea** (*Motivated Teaching*, 2020) frames attention as the currency: teachers "budget" scarce student attention, and vocal delivery is one of the main levers for directing it. His *Lean Lessons* argument matches Lemov's economy of language: every extra sentence spends attention.

### 3.3 Practical voice guidance for a science classroom

- **Teach the routine of silence before instructions** (Lemov: *Do not talk over*): in a lab, this is a safety system, not just pedagogy. Use a fixed, rehearsed attention signal; deliver practical instructions only into silence, in formal register, with economy of language.
- **Narrate positives quietly and precisely** (Lemov's *positive framing* / *precise praise*): acknowledge process ("this table has already labelled both axes") rather than vague praise ("great job"), which Dweck's mindset research and Kluger & DeNisi's feedback meta-analysis (§7.5) both suggest can misdirect attention to the self rather than the task.
- **Protect the instrument.** Teachers are the largest group of occupational voice users and suffer voice disorders at roughly double the rate of the general population (Roy et al. 2004). Amplify with position (speak from where the room focuses), stillness, and silence-before-speech rather than volume; hydrate; avoid habitual shouting — which is also, per Lemov, the *weakest* register.

## 4. Speech interactions and classroom talk

### 4.1 The default pattern — IRE/IRF — and its limits

Classroom talk research (Sinclair & Coulthard 1975; Mehan 1979; Courtney Cazden, *Classroom Discourse*, 2001) shows the overwhelmingly dominant exchange structure is **I–R–E/F**: teacher **Initiates**, student **Responds**, teacher **Evaluates/gives Feedback** ("What's the charge on an electron?" — "Negative." — "Good."). IRE is efficient for fast factual review but, left as the *only* pattern, it caps student talk at short answers and lets the teacher do all the cognitive work in the third turn.

The research consensus is not to abolish IRF but to **upgrade the F-move**: instead of evaluating and moving on, the third turn should probe, extend, or redirect — "Why negative?", "Say more", "Do you agree, Amir?" (see talk moves below).

### 4.2 Wait time — Mary Budd Rowe

Mary Budd Rowe's programme (1972; summarised in Rowe 1986, "Wait Time: Slowing Down May Be a Way of Speeding Up," *Journal of Teacher Education*) is one of the most replicated findings in classroom research, and it originated in *science* classrooms:

- Teachers' mean pause after asking a question was **under one second**; the pause after a student answer was similarly short.
- Extending both pauses to **3 seconds or more** produced: student responses 300–700% longer; more speculative, evidence-based reasoning; more student-to-student exchanges; more student questions; fewer failures to respond; and improved participation from previously quiet students. Teacher questioning also improved — fewer, better questions.
- There are **two wait times**: **Wait Time 1** (after the teacher's question, before naming a respondent) and **Wait Time 2** (after the student answers, before the teacher speaks). Wait Time 2 is the one teachers find hardest and is where much of the benefit lives — students extend and self-correct into the silence.
- Robert Stahl (1994) reframed the pause as "**think-time**," noting the target is uninterrupted silence of ~3 seconds *specifically to allow information processing*.

**Practice rule:** ask → count 3 full seconds → name a student (question first, name last, so everyone must think — see cold call, §5.3) → after their answer, hold 2–3 more seconds before you speak.

### 4.3 Dialogic teaching — Robin Alexander

Robin Alexander (*Towards Dialogic Teaching*, 2004; *A Dialogic Teaching Companion*, 2020), drawing on Bakhtin and on his five-nation comparative study (*Culture and Pedagogy*, 2001), argues classroom talk should be:

- **Collective** — teacher and students address tasks together;
- **Reciprocal** — they listen to each other and consider alternative views;
- **Supportive** — students can speak without fear of embarrassment over "wrong" answers;
- **Cumulative** — participants build on their own and each other's contributions into coherent lines of thinking;
- **Purposeful** — the teacher steers the talk toward specific curricular goals.

A large randomised trial of Alexander's dialogic-teaching programme (Alexander 2018, "Developing dialogic teaching: genesis, process, trial," *Research Papers in Education*; delivered in Year 5 classrooms) found approximately two months' additional progress in English, maths, and science after a two-term intervention — evidence that structured talk training changes attainment, not just atmosphere. Note the model is *dialogic*, not merely "discussion": the teacher's steering (purposefulness) is integral.

### 4.4 Exploratory talk and ground rules — Neil Mercer

Neil Mercer's *Thinking Together* programme (Mercer & Littleton, *Dialogue and the Development of Children's Thinking*, 2007; Mercer, Dawes, Wegerif & Sams 2004 for the science-specific trial) distinguishes three types of student group talk:

- **Disputational** — competitive; "yes it is / no it isn't"; no reasoning shared.
- **Cumulative** — uncritical agreement; adding without evaluating.
- **Exploratory** — partners engage *critically but constructively*: reasons given, challenges justified, ideas offered as hypotheses. Only exploratory talk reliably improves reasoning and science outcomes; Mercer's trials showed classes taught explicit **ground rules for talk** (everyone contributes, reasons are asked for, the group seeks agreement) improved both group reasoning and *individual* scores on reasoning tests and science attainment measures.

**Implication:** never assume pair/group discussion is productive by default. Teach, display, and enforce ground rules; give talk tasks a product ("agree one answer and one reason you'll be asked for"); and keep them short and accountable.

### 4.5 Talk moves — Michaels & O'Connor

Sarah Michaels and Catherine O'Connor's **Accountable Talk** work (Michaels, O'Connor & Resnick 2008; O'Connor & Michaels 1996 on "revoicing") supplies the concrete teacher moves that upgrade the IRF third turn:

- **Revoice**: "So you're saying the ice pulls heat *from* the drink — have I got that right?" (dignifies and clarifies a half-formed idea).
- **Restate**: "Who can repeat what Jamal just said in their own words?" (forces the room to listen to peers).
- **Add on**: "Who can add to that?"
- **Agree/disagree with reasons**: "Do you agree with Priya's prediction? Why?"
- **Press for reasoning**: "What's your evidence?" / "Why does that follow?"
- **Wait time** (they include Rowe's pause as a core move).

In science these map directly onto argumentation practice (claim–evidence–reasoning), which Jonathan Osborne's work on argumentation in science education (Osborne, Erduran & Simon 2004) shows must be explicitly taught and orchestrated, not hoped for.

### 4.6 Think–pair–share and structured pairing

Frank Lyman (1981) introduced **Think–Pair–Share** precisely as a wait-time-and-rehearsal device: private think time → oral rehearsal with a partner → whole-class sampling. Used before cold calling on a hard question, it raises the floor of answer quality and lowers the social risk of speaking. Keep the phases tight and timed, give the question in writing, and *cold call the share* so pairs know rehearsal is accountable.

**Ratio rule of thumb (Lemov):** aim to maximise "ratio" — the proportion of the room *thinking hard* at any moment. Whole-class talk where one student speaks and 29 relax is low-ratio unless combined with wait time, cold call, restate moves, and everyone-writes.

## 5. Questioning

### 5.1 How many questions?

The process–product literature is unusually specific here. Rosenshine (2012) reports the underlying studies:

- In a study of junior-high mathematics teachers (Evertson, Anderson, Anderson & Brophy 1980), **the most effective teachers asked an average of 24 questions during a 50-minute period, versus 8.6 for the least effective** — roughly one question every two minutes vs. one every six.
- The most effective teachers also asked **six times as many process questions** ("explain how you worked that out") as the least effective, alongside plentiful product questions ("what's the answer?").
- Stallings & Kaskowitz (1974) and Coker, Lorentz & Coker (1980) similarly found frequency of academic questioning correlated with achievement gains.

**Practical target:** in a 60-minute science lesson expect to ask **on the order of 30–50 academic questions** (many rapid, some deep), with *every* new-content segment followed by a question burst rather than "any questions?". The number is a means to Rosenshine's principle 6: questions are how you check, and principle 7: an ~80% success rate during guided practice tells you the pitch is right — much lower means re-teach; near 100% means the questions are too easy to be generative.

### 5.2 What types of questions?

- **Balance lower-order and higher-order deliberately.** Redfield & Rousseau's (1981) meta-analysis found higher-cognitive questions produced meaningfully better achievement, while Winne's (1979) review found little difference — the fair reading (Brophy & Good 1986) is that *both matter for different jobs*: lower-order questions build and verify the factual base (and dominate effective teachers' review phases); higher-order questions ("why," "what would happen if," "how do these differ") drive the elaboration that Willingham and Shimamura identify as the engine of memory. Craig & colleagues' work on **elaborative interrogation** (Pressley et al. 1987; rated a promising technique by Dunlosky et al. 2013) supports routine "why would that be true?" questioning once base facts are in place.
- **Use a depth ladder, not a checklist.** Bloom's taxonomy (Bloom et al. 1956; revised by Anderson & Krathwohl 2001) and Biggs & Collis's **SOLO taxonomy** (1982: prestructural → unistructural → multistructural → relational → extended abstract) are best used to *sequence* question chains within a topic: retrieve the parts (unistructural/multistructural) → relate them ("how does concentration affect rate *and why*?") → extend ("would this hold at 0 K? in a liquid?").
- **Ask process questions after correct answers, not only after errors.** Evertson's finding above; also Rosenshine: effective teachers ask students to explain *how* they arrived at correct answers, which rehearses the procedure for everyone.
- **Design diagnostic (hinge) questions around known misconceptions.** Wiliam (2011, 2015) defines a **hinge question** as a planned multiple-choice question at a decision point (~mid-lesson): every student answers in under a minute, the teacher interprets responses in under 15–30 seconds, and each *wrong option corresponds to a known misconception* so the wrong answer tells you *what* to fix. Crucially, design so students cannot get the right answer for the wrong reason. In science, Driver's catalogued misconceptions (§8.1) are the distractor bank.
- **Include prediction questions before demonstrations** — White & Gunstone's Predict–Observe–Explain (§8.3) and Crouch et al. (2004), who showed students who *predicted* a demo's outcome learned significantly more than students who merely watched it.

### 5.3 How to deliver questions

The delivery findings converge on one principle — **every question should make every student think** — and a small toolkit achieves it:

1. **Question first, pause, then name** (Rowe's Wait Time 1). Naming first releases everyone else.
2. **No hands up for answering** (Wiliam 2011): hands-up lets a confident minority do all the retrieval while the rest opt out; Wiliam's rule is hands up only to *ask* a question. Select respondents by **cold call** or randomisation (named lolly sticks etc. — with the caveat that Wiliam notes teacher judgement in *targeting* can beat pure randomness once trust is established).
3. **Cold call warmly and predictably** (Lemov, TLAC): cold call is a *culture of inclusion* ("I expect everyone to be ready; your thinking matters"), not a gotcha. Delivered in formal, warm register, with wait time, it becomes normal within weeks. Dallimore, Hertenstein & Platt (2013, and 2019 follow-up) found that in classes with high cold-calling, **voluntary participation rose over time and student comfort with participation increased** — the opposite of the anxiety objection, provided the climate is supportive.
4. **Pose–Pause–Pounce–Bounce** (popularised by Ross Morrison McGill; endorsed by Wiliam as a wait-time-plus-redirection routine): pose to all → pause (3s+) → pounce (name a student) → **bounce** the answer to another ("Do you agree? Build on it.") — which operationalises Michaels & O'Connor's *restate/add-on* moves and keeps listening accountable.
5. **No opt out** (Lemov): "I don't know" is met with support then return — ask another student or supply a cue, then **come back to the first student to say the full answer**. The sequence ends with the original student succeeding, which both rehearses the content and teaches that disengagement doesn't remove the demand.
6. **Follow up answers, right or wrong** (upgraded F-move, §4.1): "Why?", "How do you know?", "Say it again using the word *resultant*." Sherrington calls the last move **"say it again, better"** — first answers are drafts; asking for a second, more precise version rehearses academic register.
7. **Whole-class simultaneous response for coverage questions**: mini-whiteboards, ABCD finger votes, or choral response (Engelmann's DI uses choral response with a clear signal to get response-per-minute rates far above hand-raising). Use these when you need *evidence about everyone* (§7.2), and single-student cold call when you want depth and dialogue.

### 5.4 Questioning ratio across the lesson

A research-consistent distribution across a 60-minute lesson:

- **Starter (retrieval)**: 4–8 written retrieval questions, everyone answers (testing effect, §2.6).
- **During explanation**: a checking question every 2–4 minutes of teacher talk (Rosenshine's small steps + check), mostly lower-order + "why" chasers; delivered cold-call with wait time.
- **Mid-lesson**: 1 planned **hinge question**, all-student response, misconception-based distractors.
- **Guided practice**: rapid product + process questions targeting ~80% success; bounce and press moves.
- **Close**: 1–3 exit-ticket questions in writing, collected (evidence for tomorrow's plan, §7.4).

## 6. The visualiser

The visualiser (document camera) is the highest-leverage piece of technology in a science classroom because it makes three research-backed practices cheap: **live modelling**, **worked examples with fading**, and **whole-class scrutiny of student work**. The key practitioner authority is Adam Boxer; the underpinning science is Sweller, Renkl, Paivio, and Mayer.

### 6.1 Live modelling beats pre-prepared perfection — Adam Boxer

Adam Boxer (*Teaching Secondary Science: A Complete Guide*, 2021; and his widely used CogSciSci work) argues the visualiser should largely replace the pre-made slide for anything procedural or constructed:

- **Build diagrams and notes live, in front of students, narrating as you go.** A finished diagram on a slide arrives all-at-once and overloads a novice (high element interactivity, no guidance about where to look). Drawing it live **segments** the information (Mayer's segmenting principle), **directs attention** to exactly one element at a time (signalling), and lets you interleave questions at each stage ("before I draw the next arrow — which direction will the force act? Whiteboards.").
- **Slides are for images and stimuli; the visualiser is for thinking.** Boxer's rule of thumb: anything you want students to *be able to produce* (a method, a graph, a dot-and-cross diagram, a 6-mark answer) should be *produced* in front of them, imperfectly and deliberately, with the decision points narrated.
- **Narrate the metacognition, not just the steps** (this is the *cognitive apprenticeship* principle of Collins, Brown & Newman 1989 — make expert thinking visible): "First I check the units — I always do this before anything else — because…". Rosenshine (principle 4) explicitly includes teacher *think-alouds* as models.

### 6.2 Worked examples, example–problem pairs, and fading

Under the visualiser, run the worked-example literature as a routine:

1. **I do**: full worked example, narrated (Sweller & Cooper 1985 — studying worked examples beats problem solving for novices).
2. **We do**: a second, structurally identical example completed by cold-called student steps ("what's my first move? …why?").
3. **Faded examples**: give a partially completed example with final steps missing, then more missing (**backward fading** — Renkl, Atkinson & Große 2004 found fading beats abrupt example→problem switches).
4. **You do**: independent problems (expertise-reversal, §2.2, says don't linger on models once students are secure).
5. Prompt **self-explanation** throughout ("why did I divide by 1000 here?") — Chi et al. (1989) and Renkl (1997) found the learning from worked examples depends on the quality of learners' self-explanations, and prompting them raises it.

The **gradual release of responsibility** model — "I do, we do, you do" — traces to Pearson & Gallagher (1983) and is elaborated by Fisher & Frey (*Better Learning Through Structured Teaching*, 2008), who add the crucial *collaborative* phase between guided and independent.

### 6.3 Dual coding and multimedia rules — Paivio, Mayer, Caviglioli

- **Dual coding theory** (Allan Paivio, 1971, 1986): verbal and visual information are processed in partly separate channels; combining a spoken explanation with a *relevant, congruent* diagram creates two retrieval routes. Oliver Caviglioli (*Dual Coding With Teachers*, 2019) translates this for classrooms: simple, teacher-drawn diagrams, timelines, and graphic organisers beat decorative imagery.
- **Richard Mayer's multimedia principles** (*Multimedia Learning*, 3rd ed. 2021; Clark & Mayer, *e-Learning and the Science of Instruction*) govern how to use the visualiser and slides:
  - **Coherence**: cut decorative images, background music, clip-art — extraneous material reliably harms learning (the seductive-details effect).
  - **Signalling**: cue what matters (point, circle, underline live — the visualiser makes this natural).
  - **Redundancy**: **do not read aloud paragraphs displayed on screen**; narration + graphic beats narration + graphic + on-screen text.
  - **Spatial/temporal contiguity**: put labels *on* the diagram, next to the part they name; speak the explanation *while* drawing, not before or after.
  - **Segmenting**: learner-paced chunks beat continuous presentation — build the diagram stage by stage with a check between stages.
  - **Modality**: explain a diagram in speech rather than in written captions when both are presented together.

### 6.4 Visualiser for student work and practicals

- **Show-call** (Lemov): project a student's actual work — celebrated or (with a built "culture of error," §7.3) analysed for improvement. Comparing a strong and a developing answer side-by-side operationalises what Daisy Christodoulou (*Making Good Progress?*, 2016) argues about exemplars: quality is easier to *show* than to describe in rubric language.
- **Live-mark a paragraph or calculation under the camera** while students apply the same checks to their own — modelling self-assessment (Sadler 1989: students must hold a concept of quality similar to the teacher's; that concept is transmitted by exemplars and modelling, not rubric prose).
- **Demonstrate small apparatus and techniques under the camera** (reading a meniscus, setting up a micrometer, a microscope field of view) so 30 students see what only 3 would see around a front bench — then run **integrated, step-numbered visual instructions** for practicals, which the CLT literature on split attention supports and which Boxer and colleagues have popularised as "integrated instructions" for reducing load during practical work (Paterson 2019, *Education in Chemistry*).

## 7. Checks for understanding

### 7.1 Why checking is non-negotiable — Black, Wiliam, Nuthall

- **Black & Wiliam, "Assessment and Classroom Learning" (1998) and *Inside the Black Box* (1998)** reviewed ~250 studies and concluded formative assessment produces gains among the largest documented for any intervention (they reported typical effect sizes of roughly 0.4–0.7), *and* that it particularly helps lower attainers. Wiliam's *Embedded Formative Assessment* (2011) organises practice into five strategies: (1) clarifying and sharing learning intentions and success criteria; (2) engineering effective discussions and tasks that **elicit evidence of learning**; (3) feedback that moves learning forward; (4) students as instructional resources for one another; (5) students as owners of their own learning.
- **Graham Nuthall (*The Hidden Lives of Learners*, 2007)** — from decades of recording individual students with microphones through entire units — found: students already know **40–50%** of what a teacher plans to teach (but *different* students know *different* parts); a student typically needs to encounter the **complete information about a concept on at least three separate occasions** to durably learn it; about **a quarter of what each student learns is unique to them**; and much of classroom life runs through a peer culture invisible to the teacher. Consequences: whole-class impressions are unreliable, checking must sample *individuals* across the room, and key ideas must be planned to recur (3+ spaced encounters) within a unit.
- **Rosenshine's principle 6** is explicit that "are there any questions?" is *not* a check: effective teachers check by asking questions and sampling work, not by inviting self-report.

### 7.2 All-student response systems — the workhorse

Because Nuthall shows the visible minority misleads you, the default check must gather evidence from **everyone**:

- **Mini-whiteboards**: every student writes; boards up on signal ("3–2–1, show me" — Lemov's *standardise the format*: same orientation, big writing, hold to chest until signal, so scanning is fast and answers are independent). Best for short calculations, symbol equations, diagrams, one-word/one-line answers.
- **ABCD cards or finger voting** for hinge questions (Wiliam 2011): a well-designed MCQ with misconception distractors, all students respond simultaneously, teacher decision in <30 seconds: move on / re-teach a sub-group / re-teach all.
- **Everybody writes** (Lemov): a written 1–3 sentence answer before any discussion — raises ratio and gives you 30 data points to circulate over.
- **Choral response** (Engelmann) for high-frequency factual automaticity (symbols, valencies, definitions) — with a crisp signal so responses are simultaneous (a straggler echo lets students copy rather than retrieve).
- **Circulation with purpose** (Lemov's *tracking, not watching*): decide *in advance* what error you're hunting during independent practice; annotate lightly; select 2–3 pieces for show-call.

### 7.3 Culture of error

Checking only works if students expose their real thinking. Lemov (**Culture of Error**) and Alexander (*supportive* talk, §4.3) converge: normalise error as information ("thank you — that mistake is exactly the one I'd expect, and here's why it's tempting"), praise error-revealing behaviour, never mock, and respond to wrong answers with genuine curiosity ("interesting — walk me through it"). Wiliam adds the assessment version: separate the *evidence-gathering* moment from grading entirely; a checked-for-understanding lesson is low-stakes by design. Carol Dweck's mindset work supplies the framing language (praise process and strategy, not ability), while Kluger & DeNisi (§7.5) supply the warning about self-focused feedback.

### 7.4 Exit tickets and responsive re-planning

Wiliam (2011) frames the exit ticket as the bridge between lessons: 1–3 questions targeting today's core idea (at least one transfer/why question, not just recall), answered individually in the final 3–5 minutes, **read by the teacher before planning the next lesson**, and acted on visibly ("yesterday half of us multiplied when we should have divided — that's our first example today"). The action, not the slip, is the intervention. Sherrington and Wiliam both warn against exit tickets as ritual compliance: if the results never change the next lesson, stop collecting them.

### 7.5 Feedback that moves learning forward

- **Hattie & Timperley (2007, "The Power of Feedback")**: feedback is among the most powerful influences on achievement *and* among the most variable. Effective feedback answers three questions — *Where am I going? How am I going? Where to next?* — and operates at the level of **task, process, and self-regulation**; feedback about the **self** ("clever girl!") is the least effective level.
- **Kluger & DeNisi (1996)** meta-analysed 607 effect sizes and found **about 38% of feedback interventions *reduced* performance** — chiefly those directing attention to the self rather than the task. Praise, grades-with-comments (where the grade eclipses the comment — Ruth Butler 1988 showed comments-only beat grades and grades+comments), and vague evaluation are the risk zone.
- **Wiliam's rule**: feedback should be **more work for the recipient than the donor**, and should be "a recipe for future action" — e.g., "two of these five conclusions confuse correlation with causation; find them and rewrite," followed by lesson time to act on it. Feedback without a required response is broadcast, not feedback.
- **Sadler (1989)** supplies the theory: closing the gap requires the student to (a) know the goal, (b) compare current work to it, (c) act — so exemplars, modelling (§6.4), and structured redraft time are part of the feedback system, not extras.

## 8. Science-specific pedagogy

### 8.1 Misconceptions are the terrain — Rosalind Driver and conceptual change

- **Rosalind Driver** (*The Pupil as Scientist?*, 1983; Driver, Squires, Rushworth & Wood-Robinson, *Making Sense of Secondary Science*, 1994) established that children arrive with coherent, experience-based intuitive theories — heavier objects fall faster; current is "used up" around a circuit; plants get their mass from the soil; cold "gets in" — and that these frameworks are **resistant to instruction** because they work well enough in daily life. *Making Sense of Secondary Science* catalogues them domain by domain and remains the single best planning resource: **plan the lesson against the specific misconception, not just the target idea.**
- **Conceptual change** (Posner, Strike, Hewson & Gertzog 1982): replacing a framework requires the student to find it *unsatisfying* (a discrepant event, a failed prediction) and the scientific idea *intelligible, plausible, and fruitful*. Merely stating the correct idea leaves the old framework intact underneath — Nuthall's recordings show students assimilating new teaching *into* prior misconceptions.
- **Michelene Chi** (2005, 2008) adds that the hardest misconceptions are **ontological miscategorisations** (treating heat, current, or natural selection as a *substance/thing* rather than a *process/emergent pattern*), which is why "flows of stuff" language needs care.
- **Practical toolkit**: elicit predictions first (POE, §8.3); use hinge questions with misconception distractors (§5.2); use **refutation-style explanation** — explicitly state the misconception, refute it, give the scientific account (a robust finding from the refutation-text literature, e.g. Tippett 2010 review); and *expect re-teaching* — one exposure does not dislodge a framework (Nuthall's 3-encounter finding).

### 8.2 Practical work — Abrahams & Millar

- **Ian Abrahams & Robin Millar (2008, "Does practical work really work?", *International Journal of Science Education*)** observed 25 typical secondary science practical lessons and found practical work was generally effective at getting students to **do things with objects** ("domain of observables") but largely ineffective at getting them to **think with ideas** ("domain of ideas") — because tasks rarely required linking observations to concepts, and teachers rarely built in that linkage. "Hands-on" was not "minds-on."
- **Robin Millar's effectiveness framework** (Millar, Abrahams & colleagues; also Tiberghien): a practical is effective at level 1 if students do what was intended, at level 2 if they *learn* what was intended — and level 2 requires explicit design: a clear purpose statement, a prediction or question the practical answers, and structured post-practical discussion that connects data to the idea.
- **Design rules that follow:** state the *ideas-purpose* up front ("we're doing this to decide between these two predictions"); keep procedures low-load (integrated instructions, §6.4; pre-practical demonstration under the visualiser; equipment routines); **do the thinking before and after the doing** — predictions before, structured "so what did the data say about the idea?" talk after; and prefer a *purposeful demo + focused practical* over ritual whole-class practicals when the idea, not the technique, is the goal.

### 8.3 Predict–Observe–Explain and interactive demonstrations

- **White & Gunstone (*Probing Understanding*, 1992)** formalised **POE**: students commit to a **prediction** (privately, in writing, with a reason), **observe** the event, then **explain** — reconciling any conflict between prediction and observation. The written commitment is what makes the discrepant event bite (cognitive conflict per Posner et al.).
- **Crouch, Fagen, Callan & Mazur (2004, *American Journal of Physics*)** tested this experimentally with physics demonstrations: students who **predicted** the outcome before observing learned significantly more than students who passively watched — and passive watching was barely better than not seeing the demo at all. Discussion after prediction added further gains.
- **Eric Mazur's Peer Instruction** (*Peer Instruction: A User's Manual*, 1997; Crouch & Mazur 2001) is the scaled version: pose a conceptual MCQ (a *ConcepTest*) → individual commit (vote) → **if 30–70% correct, students discuss with a neighbour who answered differently** → revote → teacher explanation. A decade of Harvard data showed large gains in conceptual understanding (Force Concept Inventory) without sacrificing problem-solving. This slots naturally into the hinge-question moment of a school science lesson.

### 8.4 Guidance beats discovery for novices

- **Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006, "Why Minimal Guidance During Instruction Does Not Work," *Educational Psychologist*)**: minimally guided approaches (pure discovery, unguided inquiry, problem-based learning as sole diet) ignore working-memory limits and consistently underperform explicit instruction for novices.
- **Klahr & Nigam (2004, *Psychological Science*)**: direct instruction of the control-of-variables strategy outperformed discovery learning, *and* — countering the "discovery transfers better" claim — students taught directly transferred the skill to judging science-fair posters just as well or better.
- **Nuance:** Furtak, Seidel, Iverson & Briggs's (2012) meta-analysis found **teacher-guided inquiry** outperforms both unguided inquiry and some traditional instruction — the operative variable is *guidance*, not the label. Alfieri, Brooks, Aldrich & Tenenbaum (2011) similarly: unassisted discovery does not benefit learners; **enhanced discovery** (with scaffolds, worked examples, feedback) can. The synthesis for a science department: explicit teaching and worked examples to build knowledge; carefully scaffolded investigations to *apply* it; genuine open inquiry reserved for late-stage, high-knowledge contexts (expertise reversal, §2.2).

### 8.5 Vocabulary and reading in science

- Science is the most vocabulary-dense subject in school; the classic estimate (Yager 1983) is that a science course introduces more new words than a foreign-language course. **Alex Quigley (*Closing the Vocabulary Gap*, 2018)** synthesises the vocabulary literature into a teachable routine: explicitly teach selected Tier 2/3 words (Beck, McKeown & Kucan's tiers — *Bringing Words to Life*, 2002); teach **morphology and etymology** (*photo-synthesis*, *exo-thermic*, *homeo-stasis* — the Greek/Latin roots do explanatory work in science); and give multiple encounters in speech and writing.
- **Practice moves:** introduce the word *after* the concept where possible (concept first, label second — Engelmann); require the word in answers ("say it again with *displacement* in it" — §5.3); choral-decode new polysyllabic terms once (Raichura's routine: my turn/your turn); keep a visible, cumulative key-word bank; and design retrieval starters that retrieve *words with meanings*, not just definitions verbatim.

### 8.6 Retrieval, spacing, and interleaving across the unit

- **Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan & Willingham (2013, "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques")** rated ten techniques: **practice testing** and **distributed practice** received the highest utility ratings; elaborative interrogation, self-explanation, and interleaving were promising; **highlighting, rereading, and summarising (as typically done) rated low**. This single paper should shape both lesson starters and the study habits you teach.
- **Roediger & Karpicke (2006)**: the testing effect — retrieval beats restudy for long-term retention, and the advantage *grows* with delay. **Karpicke & Blunt (2011, *Science*)**: retrieval practice outperformed elaborative concept mapping on science texts — even on *inference* questions, and even when judged by students as less effective (learners' metacognition misleads them; cf. Bjork's "desirable difficulties" — Bjork & Bjork 2011).
- **Cepeda, Pashler, Vul, Wixted & Rohrer (2006; 2008)**: spacing study sessions beats massing, with optimal gaps scaling with the retention interval (roughly 10–20% of the interval).
- **Rohrer & Taylor (2007)**: interleaving problem *types* (mixing rate, moles, and energy calculations rather than blocking) hurts immediate performance but improves delayed test performance — because students must *choose* the method, not just execute it. Build mixed-topic practice into the independent phase of later lessons in a unit.

## 10. What NOT to do

The research base is as clear about pitfalls as about practices:

- **Learning styles matching.** Pashler, McDaniel, Rohrer & Bjork (2008) found no credible evidence that matching instruction to diagnosed "styles" (VAK etc.) improves learning. Use dual coding for *everyone*; choose the modality that fits the *content*.
- **The "learning pyramid" / Dale's cone percentages** ("we remember 10% of what we read, 90% of what we teach…") — the numbers are fabricated; no study supports them (Letrud & Hernes 2018 traced and debunked the sources). The *kernel* (generation beats passive reception) is real and better cited via Roediger, Karpicke, and Shimamura.
- **Unguided discovery for novices.** Kirschner, Sweller & Clark (2006); Klahr & Nigam (2004); Alfieri et al. (2011). Guidance first; open inquiry only with strong prior knowledge.
- **"Any questions?" / "Does everyone understand?" as a check.** Rosenshine (2012) and Lemov (*Reject Self-Report*) both name this explicitly: self-report is worthless as evidence; ask content questions of everyone instead.
- **Hands-up-only questioning.** Wiliam (2011): it samples the confident and lets the rest opt out — the same few students answer while the class's actual understanding stays hidden (Nuthall).
- **Reading slides aloud / text-heavy slides.** Mayer's redundancy and coherence principles; the transient split of attention harms exactly the students you're trying to help.
- **Grading everything / grades with comments.** Butler (1988): grades wiped out the benefit of comments; Kluger & DeNisi (1996): a third of feedback backfires, especially self-focused feedback. Keep in-lesson checking gradeless.
- **Ritual practicals without an ideas-purpose.** Abrahams & Millar (2008): hands-on without minds-on produces recipe-following, not learning. Every practical needs a question it answers and structured post-practical linkage to the idea.
- **Highlighting and rereading as taught study habits.** Dunlosky et al. (2013) rate both low-utility; teach retrieval and spacing instead — and tell students *why*, because Karpicke & Blunt showed learners' intuitions favour exactly the weaker strategies.
- **Judging the lesson by engagement.** Coe's "poor proxies for learning" (busy, motivated, attentive ≠ learning) and Nuthall's hidden lives both warn: the only trustworthy signal is *evidence of thinking* gathered from all students — which is what §7 exists to provide.
