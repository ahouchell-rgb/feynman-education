## TL;DR — the honest version

1. **No job or freelance rate reaches $1M in this timeframe from a standing start.** The math forces you into *owning equity in something that can be sold*. A profitable software business sells for roughly **4–5× its annual profit** [STRONG]; a content/education business for less. That sale — not the monthly income — is what creates the seven-figure number.

2. **Base rates are brutal and you must internalize them.** Of 6,525 software companies tracked from their first dollar of revenue, only **~13% reached $1M ARR within 3 years and ~25% within 5 years** [DIRECTIONAL]. Most micro-SaaS never clear $1k/month [DIRECTIONAL]. In the creator economy, the **median creator earns ~$3k per campaign while the average is $11.4k** — and the **top 10% capture 62% of all creator payments** [VERIFIED]. These are winner-take-most games.

3. **Your specific edge changes the odds.** The base rates above are for *average* entrants — most of whom can do only one of {build, sell, teach}. You claim all three. The people who actually win these games are disproportionately the ones who can build a product *and* distribute it themselves. That's your unfair advantage, and the whole strategy below is built to exploit it.

4. **The winning structure is a stack, not a single bet:**
   - **Cash engine** (months 0–6): a *productized service* / AI-automation offer → pays your bills, forces you to talk to paying customers, and reveals what software people will actually pay for.
   - **Distribution** (continuous): build an audience *in public* by teaching what you're learning → this is the asset that makes everything else cheaper and is the single biggest predictor of success for solo founders.
   - **Equity** (months 6–36): turn the most-repeated service request into a software product you can sell once and charge for forever → this is the part that can actually hit $1M on exit.

5. **Run it as a portfolio of cheap shots on goal, not one all-or-nothing bet** [ANALYSIS]. Because any single product has a low hit rate, the rational strategy is many small, fast, cheap attempts — kill the duds in weeks, pour everything into the one that gets traction.

## Part 1 — The realistic math (read this before getting excited)

**What "$1M net worth" actually requires.** There are only two honest routes:
- **Accumulate** $1M in cash from profit (e.g. a business netting $200k/yr for ~5–7 years after tax and living costs), or
- **Build and sell an asset.** A SaaS doing **$250k–$1M in annual profit sells for roughly 4.5× that profit** [STRONG] — so a business netting ~$250k/yr can sell for **~$1.1M**. This is the faster, more realistic seven-figure path for a solo builder.

Key verified facts about the exit market:
- Most software acquisitions happen at **enterprise value under $10M** — this is a real, liquid market for small businesses, not a fantasy [VERIFIED].
- Buyers **anchor valuation on profit, not revenue** (unless you're at exceptional scale with strong growth/retention) [VERIFIED]. *Implication: a "growth at all costs, no profit" plan does not sell. Profitability is the product.*
- **Profitable businesses get more offers and sell faster** than unprofitable ones [VERIFIED].
- A widely-cited "sell for 3–5× ARR" rule was **fact-check rejected** [VERIFIED as false] — it confuses revenue with profit. Small SaaS sells at a multiple of **profit (SDE/EBITDA)**, typically **~1.7× under $50k profit, ~2.9× at $50k–$250k, ~4.5× at $250k–$1M** [STRONG]. The multiple *rises with size and profitability* — which is why getting to real profit matters more than vanity revenue.

**Time-to-revenue reality** [DIRECTIONAL, from a 6,525-company cohort]:
- ~3% reach $1M ARR in under a year
- ~13% within 3 years
- ~25% within 5 years
- ~50% within 10 years

So a 3–7 year horizon to $1M is **achievable but top-quartile** — it requires either an above-average product *or* (more reliably) multiple attempts so you get more pulls on the slot machine.

**The survivorship-bias warning.** Every "$20k/month indie hacker" thread you read is a survivor. The median micro-SaaS makes a few hundred dollars a month and most never break $1k [DIRECTIONAL]. Twitter/X and YouTube systematically show you the 1% and hide the 99%. Plan for the median; let the upside be a bonus.

## Part 2 — The paths, compared

### Path A — Bootstrapped micro-SaaS / software product  *(the equity engine)*
- **Why it fits you:** you can build, so your main cost is *time*, not money. Capital needed is genuinely <$500 to start.
- **Upside:** this is the only path here with a clean route to a seven-figure *exit*. Sells at ~4.5× profit once meaningful [STRONG].
- **Reality:** most never get traction; ~75% don't hit $1M ARR even within 5 years [DIRECTIONAL]. The bottleneck is almost never code — it's **distribution and picking a problem people pay to solve**. Builders who can't market mostly fail.
- **Verdict:** highest ceiling. Make it the destination, but don't start cold here with no audience and no validated problem.

### Path B — Audience-first creator + education  *(the distribution + margin engine)*
- **Why it fits you:** you can teach (the repo is literally named "feynman-education") and build an audience. Teaching coding/AI is one of the hottest, highest-willingness-to-pay niches right now.
- **Reality check** [VERIFIED]: median creator ~$3k/campaign vs $11.4k average; **top 10% take 62% of all payments**, top 1% take 21%, and concentration is *worsening* year over year. This is a power law — being median is close to worthless; being top-decile is life-changing.
- **The real value for you isn't ad/sponsor income — it's the *audience as a distribution asset*** [ANALYSIS]. An email list of even a few thousand engaged developers means every future product launches to warm demand instead of into the void. That's what de-risks Path A.
- **Verdict:** don't pursue "being a creator" for its own sake (median outcomes are poor). Pursue audience as *infrastructure* for selling products and high-ticket cohorts. A paid cohort course teaching a real skill can do $20k–$100k+ per cohort *if* you have the audience first.

### Path C — Productized service / AI-automation agency  *(the cash bridge)*
- **Why it fits you:** uses all three skills (sell + build + explain) and pays *now*, with near-zero startup capital. A "productized service" = a fixed-scope, fixed-price offer (e.g. "I'll build your customer-support AI workflow for $2k") rather than hourly consulting.
- **Reality:** high margins (you're selling your time + cheap AI tooling), but it **doesn't compound** — revenue is capped by hours and it doesn't build a sellable asset. Also note: **~95% of enterprise generative-AI pilots fail to deliver measurable P&L impact** [DIRECTIONAL, MIT NANDA study] — so position yourself around *boring, back-office automation that demonstrably saves money*, not flashy demos.
- **Verdict:** the **best starting point**. It funds your runway, forces customer conversations, and the requests you hear repeatedly become your SaaS idea. Use it as a bridge, not a destination.

### Path D — Pure AI "wrapper" apps  *(mostly a trap, with exceptions)*
- Thin "ChatGPT-over-X" apps with no moat are getting commoditized fast; the durable economics come from **owning a workflow, proprietary data, distribution, or a specific vertical's trust** — not from the model [DIRECTIONAL]. The AI is a feature, not the business.
- **Verdict:** AI is an *ingredient* in Paths A–C, not a standalone path. Don't build a wrapper hoping the novelty carries it.

## Part 3 — The recommended strategy (synthesis)

**Stack the three engines so each de-risks the next** [ANALYSIS]:

```
            ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
            │  TEACH IN PUBLIC (audience = distribution)   │  ← continuous, from day 1
            └─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                 │ warm demand          │ credibility
                 ▼                       ▼
   ┌────────────────────────┐   ┌──────────────────────────┐
   │  PRODUCTIZED SERVICE   │──▶│   SOFTWARE PRODUCT (SaaS) │
   │  (cash now, months 0+) │   │   (equity/exit, months 6+)│
   └────────────────────────┘   └──────────────────────────┘
        funds runway &               sells at ~4.5× profit →
        reveals the product            the seven-figure outcome
```

Why this beats picking one path: the service generates cash and *tells you which product to build* (you literally hear the same request 10 times). The audience means that product launches to warm buyers instead of crickets — fixing the #1 reason solo SaaS dies. The product is the only piece that can be *sold* for a lump sum, which is how you actually reach $1M.

## Part 4 — Concrete first-90-day plan

**Weeks 1–2 — Pick a beachhead niche.**
- Choose ONE narrow audience you understand and that has money (e.g. "small law firms," "Shopify stores doing $1–10M," "real-estate teams"). Narrow beats broad — it makes both content and sales 10× easier.
- Define one painful, repetitive, *expensive* problem in that niche that AI/automation can kill.

**Weeks 2–8 — Launch the cash engine + start teaching.**
- Package a **fixed-scope, fixed-price productized service** solving that one problem (target $1,500–$5,000 per engagement).
- **Sell it manually**: direct outreach, communities, your network. Goal: land 2–3 paying clients. This validates real willingness to pay.
- Simultaneously, **publish 3×/week** teaching exactly what you're doing (build-in-public posts, short videos, a simple newsletter). Document real work; don't theorize. This compounds into the audience asset.

**Weeks 8–12 — Find the product seed.**
- Notice which part of your service you rebuild for *every* client. That repeated, copy-pasteable thing is your SaaS candidate.
- Build the smallest possible version (you can ship fast). Offer it to your existing service clients first — they're pre-validated buyers.
- Keep the service running to fund everything; let the product grow underneath it.

**Success metric at day 90:** at least one paying service client, an audience growing weekly (even 200→1,000 engaged followers/subscribers), and one concrete, validated product idea with a first user. Revenue target is modest ($2k–$10k total); the real deliverables are *validation + distribution + a product seed*.

## Part 5 — Failure modes to avoid (these kill most people)

1. **Building before selling.** Don't write a line of product code until someone has paid you for the underlying problem. The graveyard is full of polished apps no one wanted.
2. **Chasing median creator income.** Don't try to "go viral" or live off sponsorships — the data says that's a poor-median game [VERIFIED]. Use the audience to sell, not to farm ad pennies.
3. **Vanity revenue over profit.** Buyers pay for *profit* [VERIFIED]. Growth without margin doesn't sell and doesn't fund your life. Stay lean and profitable.
4. **One big bet.** Single products have low hit rates. Run a *portfolio of cheap, fast attempts*; kill anything without traction in 4–8 weeks [ANALYSIS].
5. **Flashy AI demos over boring ROI.** 95% of GenAI pilots show no P&L impact [DIRECTIONAL]. Sell measurable cost/time savings, not novelty.
6. **No distribution.** A great product with no audience is the most common silent death. The teaching/audience track isn't optional — it's the moat.
7. **Quitting at the dip.** Time-to-$1M is realistically 3–7 years with a long flat stretch before compounding kicks in [DIRECTIONAL]. The people who win are mostly the ones who didn't stop.
